1. What is ISO 27001:2022 Gap Analysis?

An ISO 27001 gap analysis is a systematic comparison between your organization's current information security posture and the requirements of the ISO 27001:2022 standard. Think of it as a health check for your security program that reveals exactly where you stand and what needs improvement before pursuing certification.

The gap analysis answers three critical questions:

  • Where are we now? Current state of security controls and processes
  • Where do we need to be? ISO 27001:2022 requirements and expectations
  • What's the gap? The delta between current state and compliance requirements
Why Gap Analysis First?

Organizations that skip gap analysis spend an average of 40% more time on implementation. The gap analysis provides a roadmap that prevents wasted effort on controls you already have while highlighting critical blind spots.

When to Conduct a Gap Analysis

Scenario Gap Analysis Purpose Typical Duration
First-time certification Baseline assessment, implementation roadmap 2-4 weeks
Transition from 2013 to 2022 Identify new controls, mapping existing controls 1-2 weeks
Post-acquisition/merger Assess inherited systems and processes 3-6 weeks
Pre-audit health check Identify gaps before certification body arrives 1 week
Annual review Continuous improvement, maintain compliance 3-5 days

2. Key Changes: ISO 27001:2013 vs 2022

The 2022 revision brought significant restructuring to Annex A. Understanding these changes is essential for anyone familiar with the previous version.

Aspect ISO 27001:2013 ISO 27001:2022
Total Controls 114 controls 93 controls
Control Domains 14 domains (A.5 - A.18) 4 themes
New Controls - 11 new controls
Merged Controls - 24 controls merged
Control Attributes Not included 5 attribute types for each control
Transition Deadline - October 31, 2025

The 11 New Controls in ISO 27001:2022

These controls reflect modern security challenges that didn't exist or weren't prominent in 2013:

Control ID Control Name Why It's New
A.5.7 Threat Intelligence Proactive threat awareness
A.5.23 Information Security for Cloud Services Cloud adoption explosion
A.5.30 ICT Readiness for Business Continuity Technology resilience focus
A.7.4 Physical Security Monitoring Surveillance and detection
A.8.9 Configuration Management Baseline security settings
A.8.10 Information Deletion Data lifecycle management
A.8.11 Data Masking Privacy and data protection
A.8.12 Data Leakage Prevention DLP requirements
A.8.16 Monitoring Activities Detection and SIEM
A.8.23 Web Filtering Content filtering controls
A.8.28 Secure Coding Application security focus
Transition Deadline

All certified organizations must transition to ISO 27001:2022 by October 31, 2025. Certifications under the 2013 version will not be valid after this date.

3. Gap Analysis Methodology

A structured methodology ensures consistent, repeatable assessments. Here's a proven five-phase approach:

Phase 1: Scope Definition

  • Define ISMS boundaries (business units, locations, systems)
  • Identify key stakeholders and process owners
  • Gather existing documentation (policies, procedures, risk assessments)
  • Establish assessment timeline and resources

Phase 2: Document Review

  • Review existing ISMS documentation against ISO 27001 requirements
  • Assess policy completeness and currency
  • Evaluate procedure alignment with controls
  • Check evidence of implementation and records

Phase 3: Control Assessment

For each of the 93 Annex A controls, evaluate:

  • Implementation Status: Not implemented, Partially, Fully implemented
  • Evidence Quality: None, Informal, Documented, Proven effective
  • Gap Severity: Critical, High, Medium, Low
  • Remediation Effort: Quick win, Moderate, Significant project

Phase 4: Stakeholder Interviews

  • Interview process owners to validate document review findings
  • Observe actual practices vs documented procedures
  • Identify undocumented but effective controls
  • Uncover cultural and awareness gaps

Phase 5: Reporting and Roadmap

  • Compile findings into gap analysis report
  • Prioritize gaps by risk and remediation effort
  • Create remediation timeline with milestones
  • Estimate resource requirements and budget
Best Practice: Evidence-Based Assessment

Never rely solely on interviews. For each control, request evidence: screenshots, logs, policy documents, training records. "We do that" isn't evidence; a signed procedure with audit trail is.

4. The Four Annex A Themes (93 Controls)

ISO 27001:2022 restructured the 93 controls into four logical themes. Understanding this structure helps organize your assessment and responsibilities.

Organizational Controls

37 controls

Policies, roles, responsibilities, asset management, access control, supplier relationships, incident management, business continuity, compliance

People Controls

8 controls

Screening, employment terms, awareness training, disciplinary process, termination, remote working, event reporting

Physical Controls

14 controls

Physical perimeters, entry controls, securing offices, monitoring, protecting against threats, equipment security, secure disposal, clear desk

Technological Controls

34 controls

Endpoint devices, access rights, authentication, malware, backups, logging, monitoring, network security, cryptography, secure development, testing

5. Most Commonly Failed Controls

Based on analysis of hundreds of certification audits, these controls have the highest failure rates. Pay special attention during your gap analysis:

Critical A.5.7 - Threat Intelligence

Why it fails: Organizations claim they "monitor threats" but lack structured processes. Reading security news isn't threat intelligence.

What auditors look for:

  • Documented threat intelligence sources and feeds
  • Process for analyzing and actioning threat information
  • Evidence of threat briefings to relevant stakeholders
  • Integration with risk assessment and incident response
War Story: The "We Read The News" Defense

A financial services company confidently claimed A.5.7 compliance because their security team "follows industry news." The auditor asked for evidence of how a recent ransomware campaign affecting their sector was analyzed and actioned. Silence. The auditor found no documentation of threat sources, no analysis process, no evidence of sharing with IT teams. Result: Major non-conformity.

Critical A.6.3 - Security Awareness Training

Why it fails: Annual checkbox training with no measurement of effectiveness. No role-based training. No evidence of behavior change.

What auditors look for:

  • Training program aligned to roles and risks
  • Completion records with dates and attendee signatures
  • Assessment/testing of knowledge retention
  • Evidence of continuous reinforcement (phishing simulations, newsletters)
  • Training updates when policies or threats change

High A.8.9 - Configuration Management

Why it fails: Organizations have configurations but no documented baselines. Drift from baseline isn't monitored. No approval process for changes.

What auditors look for:

  • Documented security baseline configurations for each technology
  • Process to detect and remediate configuration drift
  • Change management integration
  • Regular configuration reviews (hardening assessments)

High A.5.23 - Cloud Security

Why it fails: Shadow IT, unclear responsibility models, treating cloud like on-premises.

What auditors look for:

  • Cloud service inventory and risk assessment
  • Clear shared responsibility documentation per provider
  • Cloud-specific security configurations and monitoring
  • Exit strategy and data portability planning

High A.8.16 - Monitoring Activities

Why it fails: Logs exist but nobody reviews them. Alerts fire but nobody responds. No defined thresholds or escalation.

What auditors look for:

  • Defined monitoring requirements (what, where, how often)
  • Alert thresholds and escalation procedures
  • Evidence of log review (signed reviews, SIEM reports)
  • Incident correlation and detection rules

Medium A.8.28 - Secure Coding

Why it fails: Developers say they follow secure coding, but no standards exist. No code review process. No security testing in CI/CD.

What auditors look for:

  • Documented secure coding standards (OWASP, SANS)
  • Security code review process with records
  • SAST/DAST integration in development pipeline
  • Developer security training specific to languages used

Medium A.5.22 - Supplier Monitoring

Why it fails: Initial assessment done, but no ongoing monitoring. No process for supplier changes or incidents.

What auditors look for:

  • Regular supplier security reviews (annual minimum)
  • Monitoring of supplier security posture changes
  • Notification requirements for supplier incidents
  • Right to audit clauses exercised

6. Gap Scoring and Prioritization

Not all gaps are equal. Use a scoring matrix to prioritize remediation efforts:

Gap Severity Matrix

Severity Criteria Remediation Priority Timeline
Critical Control not implemented; high risk exposure; will result in major NC Immediate 0-30 days
High Control partially implemented; significant gaps; likely minor NC High 30-60 days
Medium Control mostly implemented; minor gaps; observation likely Medium 60-90 days
Low Control implemented; minor improvements possible; optimization Low 90+ days

Prioritization Formula

Calculate priority score for each gap:

Priority Score = (Risk Impact × Likelihood) + Audit Probability - Implementation Effort

Where:
- Risk Impact: 1-5 (1=Low, 5=Critical)
- Likelihood: 1-5 (1=Rare, 5=Almost Certain)  
- Audit Probability: How likely auditor will check (1-3)
- Implementation Effort: Resource requirement (1-5, higher = harder)

Example Calculation:

  • A.5.7 Threat Intelligence: (4 × 3) + 3 - 3 = 12 (High priority)
  • A.8.28 Secure Coding: (3 × 2) + 2 - 4 = 4 (Medium priority)

7. Remediation Planning

Transform your gap findings into an actionable remediation plan:

Remediation Plan Template

Control Gap Description Action Required Owner Target Date Status
A.5.7 No formal threat intel process Implement threat intel feeds, create analysis procedure SOC Manager Feb 28 In Progress
A.6.3 Training lacks role-based content Develop role-specific training modules HR/Security Mar 15 Not Started
A.8.9 No baseline configurations documented Create CIS-based baselines, implement drift detection IT Ops Apr 1 Planning

Quick Wins vs. Projects

Quick Wins (1-2 weeks):

  • Documenting existing but undocumented processes
  • Updating policies with missing sections
  • Enabling existing but unused security features
  • Creating missing forms and templates

Projects (1-3 months):

  • Implementing new technology controls (DLP, SIEM)
  • Developing comprehensive training programs
  • Establishing new processes (threat intelligence, secure development)
  • Third-party risk management program build-out
Best Practice: Evidence as You Go

As you remediate each gap, immediately collect and organize evidence. Don't wait until audit preparation. Create an evidence folder structure mirroring Annex A controls.

8. War Stories: Lessons from Real Assessments

The Over-Confident CISO

A technology company's CISO was confident their existing SOC 2 controls would map directly to ISO 27001. The gap analysis revealed 34 control gaps. SOC 2 covers security, but ISO 27001 includes physical security, HR processes, and supplier management that SOC 2 doesn't require. Lesson: Never assume framework equivalence without mapping.

The Documentation Desert

A healthcare organization had excellent security practices but almost no documentation. Engineers knew what to do, but nothing was written down. The gap analysis found only 12 of 93 controls had adequate documentation. Six months of intensive documentation work followed. Lesson: Undocumented controls don't exist in an audit.

The Shadow IT Surprise

During A.5.9 (asset inventory) assessment, the gap analysis team discovered 47 SaaS applications in use that IT knew nothing about. Marketing had procurement cards. Each unapproved app was a potential A.5.23 (cloud security) violation. The scope had to be expanded significantly. Lesson: Always do discovery before assuming you know your asset universe.

The Backup That Wasn't

For A.8.13 (backup), the IT team showed beautiful backup job success reports. The gap analyst asked for restore test evidence. Last restore test? 18 months ago. A test restore was performed during the assessment and failed. Backups had been completing but were corrupted. Lesson: Backup without tested restore is just storage.

10. Next Steps: From Gap to Certification

Your gap analysis is complete. Here's what comes next:

Immediate Actions (Week 1-2)

  1. Present gap analysis findings to leadership
  2. Secure budget and resource commitment
  3. Assign control owners for each gap
  4. Prioritize quick wins for early momentum

Implementation Phase (Months 1-6)

  1. Execute remediation plan in priority order
  2. Document everything as you implement
  3. Conduct mini internal audits to verify closure
  4. Update risk assessment with control improvements

Certification Preparation (Months 6-9)

  1. Conduct full internal audit
  2. Management review meeting
  3. Address internal audit findings
  4. Select certification body and schedule audit
