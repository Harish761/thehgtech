Enterprise guide to governing ChatGPT, Copilot, and generative AI tools

1. Why AI Governance Matters

Understanding the Basics

Your employees are already using ChatGPT, Claude, and other AI tools - often without telling IT. They're pasting customer emails, contract clauses, code snippets, and financial data into these tools every single day.

Here's the problem: When your sales rep pastes a customer contract into ChatGPT to "summarize the key points," that confidential contract just became training data for OpenAI. When your developer shares proprietary code with GitHub Copilot, it potentially becomes part of suggestions for your competitors.

Think of it like this: Imagine if your employees were photocopying confidential documents and mailing them to random companies for feedback. You'd stop that immediately, right? That's essentially what's happening with ungoverned AI use - except it's invisible and happening thousands of times per day.

Real-World AI Disasters (Learn from Others' Mistakes)

Year Incident What Went Wrong
2023 Samsung semiconductor leak Engineers pasted proprietary chip designs into ChatGPT; data potentially used for training
2023 Lawyer cited fake cases Attorney filed brief with AI-hallucinated case citations that didn't exist; sanctioned by court
2024 Multiple banks banned AI JPMorgan, Goldman, others banned ChatGPT after employees shared client info
2024 Air Canada chatbot AI made up refund policy; airline forced to honor false AI promises
The Samsung Incident Explained: Samsung engineers used ChatGPT to check source code for errors and optimize code. They inadvertently leaked source code for semiconductor equipment, internal meeting notes, and proprietary test data. Samsung responded by banning ChatGPT entirely - but many employees just used it on personal devices instead.

The Technical Perspective

The rapid adoption of generative AI tools like ChatGPT, Microsoft Copilot, and Claude has created significant governance challenges. According to ISACA's 2024 survey, 75% of organizations have no formal AI policy despite widespread employee use of AI tools.

Key Risks of Ungoverned AI

  • Data Leakage: Employees inadvertently sharing confidential data with public AI services that may use inputs for training
  • Compliance Violations: AI outputs that violate regulations (GDPR for using customer data, HIPAA for health info, SOX for financial controls)
  • Intellectual Property: Using AI-generated content without understanding IP implications - who owns AI-generated code or content?
  • Bias & Discrimination: AI decisions that perpetuate harmful biases in hiring, lending, or customer service
  • Hallucinations: Relying on AI-generated false information for critical decisions (the lawyer example above)
  • Shadow AI: Employees using unapproved AI tools without IT knowledge, creating security blind spots
Key Insight: Governance is not about blocking AI adoptionâ€”it's about enabling safe, effective AI use that protects the organization while unlocking productivity benefits. The companies winning are those with clear AI policies, not those trying to ban AI entirely.

2. AI Governance Framework Components

A comprehensive AI governance framework consists of six interconnected components:

Component Purpose Key Deliverables
Strategy & Vision Define how AI supports business objectives AI strategy document, use case priorities
Policies & Standards Establish rules for AI use Acceptable use policy, data handling standards
Risk Management Identify and mitigate AI-specific risks AI risk assessment framework, risk register
Governance Structure Define oversight and decision-making AI committee, RACI matrix, escalation paths
Compliance & Ethics Ensure legal and ethical AI use Compliance checklist, ethics guidelines
Monitoring & Assurance Verify ongoing compliance Audit program, KPIs, incident response

3. AI Risk Classification

Classify AI use cases based on risk level to apply appropriate controls:

High Risk / Prohibited

Description: AI applications that pose unacceptable risks to safety, rights, or regulatory compliance.

Examples:

  • Autonomous decisions affecting employment, credit, or legal matters without human review
  • Processing sensitive personal data (health, biometric, financial) without explicit consent
  • Real-time biometric identification in public spaces
  • Social scoring or manipulation of behavior

Controls: Prohibited without extensive review and approval by legal, compliance, and executive leadership.

Medium Risk

Description: AI applications with significant impact requiring human oversight and controls.

Examples:

  • Customer-facing AI assistants and chatbots
  • AI-assisted content creation for external publication
  • Code generation with Copilot for production systems
  • Data analysis and business intelligence

Controls: Require approval from department head, human review of outputs, data handling agreements, and regular monitoring.

Low Risk / General Use

Description: AI applications for productivity with minimal organizational risk.

Examples:

  • Grammar and writing assistance (with no sensitive data)
  • Meeting summarization (internal meetings only)
  • Code explanation and learning assistance
  • Public information research

Controls: General acceptable use policy awareness, no sensitive data prohibition, training completion.

4. Acceptable Use Policies

Your AI acceptable use policy should address these critical areas:

Approved vs. Prohibited Activities

Approved Prohibited
Drafting internal communications Inputting confidential business data
Researching public information Sharing customer PII with AI tools
Code review and explanation Using AI for final decisions without review
Brainstorming and ideation Creating deepfakes or deceptive content
Learning and skill development Bypassing security controls

Data Classification for AI

  • Public Data: Can be used with any approved AI tool
  • Internal Data: Only with enterprise-licensed AI tools (e.g., Microsoft 365 Copilot with data residency)
  • Confidential Data: Only with approved on-premise or air-gapped AI solutions
  • Restricted/PII Data: Generally prohibited from AI tools; requires explicit CISO approval

5. Data Protection & Privacy

Key Data Protection Requirements

  • Data Minimization: Only share data absolutely necessary for the AI task
  • Anonymization: Remove PII before using data with external AI services
  • Vendor Agreements: Ensure AI vendors have appropriate DPAs (Data Processing Agreements)
  • Data Residency: Verify where AI providers process and store data (especially for EU data)
  • Training Data Opt-Out: Configure AI tools to not use your data for model training
Critical: Public ChatGPT, Claude, and similar free services may use your inputs for training. Use enterprise versions with appropriate data handling agreements for any business data.

6. AI Security Considerations

AI-Specific Security Risks

  • Prompt Injection: Malicious inputs that manipulate AI behavior
  • Data Exfiltration: AI tools leaking sensitive information in responses
  • Model Poisoning: Corrupted training data affecting AI outputs
  • Supply Chain Risk: Third-party AI models with unknown vulnerabilities

Security Controls

  • Deploy DLP (Data Loss Prevention) to monitor AI tool usage
  • Use enterprise AI tools with SSO and audit logging
  • Implement network controls to block unapproved AI services
  • Regular security assessments of AI integrations

7. EU AI Act Compliance

The EU AI Act is the world's first comprehensive AI regulation. Key compliance requirements:

Risk-Based Classification

Category Examples Requirements
Unacceptable Risk Social scoring, manipulation Prohibited
High Risk Employment, credit, education AI Conformity assessment, human oversight, logging
Limited Risk Chatbots, content generation Transparency requirements
Minimal Risk Spam filters, games No specific requirements

Key Obligations (Effective Feb 2025)

  • Transparency: Disclose when content is AI-generated or when users interact with AI
  • Human Oversight: Maintain human control over high-risk AI systems
  • Technical Documentation: Document AI system design, training data, and performance
  • Risk Assessment: Conduct and maintain AI risk assessments

8. Roles & Responsibilities

Role Responsibilities
AI Governance Committee Strategy, policy approval, high-risk decisions, oversight
Chief AI Officer / AI Lead Framework ownership, stakeholder coordination, reporting
CISO / Security Team AI security controls, DLP, vendor security assessment
Legal / Compliance Regulatory compliance, contracts, IP review
Data Privacy Officer GDPR/privacy compliance, data handling standards
Business Unit Leaders Use case identification, risk acceptance, training enforcement
All Employees Policy compliance, training completion, incident reporting

9. Monitoring & Oversight

Key Performance Indicators

  • Percentage of employees completing AI training
  • Number of AI use cases reviewed and approved
  • AI-related security incidents
  • Policy violations detected
  • Shadow AI usage (unapproved tools)

Audit Program

  • Quarterly review of approved AI tools and usage patterns
  • Annual AI governance framework assessment
  • Incident-triggered reviews for policy violations
  • Third-party AI vendor security assessments

10. Policy Templates

Sample Policy Statement

AI ACCEPTABLE USE POLICY

Purpose: This policy establishes guidelines for the responsible use of artificial intelligence tools by [Organization] employees.

Scope: All employees, contractors, and third parties using AI tools for [Organization] business.

Key Requirements:
1. Only approved AI tools may be used for business purposes
2. Confidential, restricted, or personal data must NOT be entered into AI tools
3. AI-generated outputs must be reviewed by humans before use in decisions
4. All AI use must comply with applicable laws and regulations
5. Violations must be reported to [contact]

Approved Tools: [List enterprise-approved AI tools]
Effective Date: [Date]
Policy Owner: [Title]
Next Steps:
  1. Inventory current AI tool usage across the organization
  2. Establish an AI governance committee with key stakeholders
  3. Draft and approve an AI acceptable use policy
  4. Deploy training for all employees
  5. Implement technical controls (DLP, approved tool list)
  6. Establish ongoing monitoring and review processes
