Threat hunting is where security gets proactive. Instead of waiting for alerts to fire, you go looking for adversaries who have already bypassed your defenses. It's detective work‚Äîforming hypotheses, collecting evidence, and finding what others missed.

I've spent countless hours hunting through logs, and here's what I've learned: the best threat hunters aren't the ones with the most tools‚Äîthey're the ones who think like attackers and understand their environment deeply.

Threat Hunting vs. Incident Response: IR is reactive‚Äîyou respond to alerts. Hunting is proactive‚Äîyou assume you're already compromised and search for evidence. The goal is to find threats that evaded detection.

Step 1: Form a Hypothesis

Every hunt starts with a question. Good hypotheses are:

  • Specific: "Attackers may be using PowerShell to download malware" vs. "There might be malware"
  • Testable: Can be proven or disproven with available data
  • Threat-informed: Based on real TTPs used by adversaries

Step 2: Collect Data

Identify what data sources you need:

  • Endpoint: Process execution, file creation, registry changes
  • Network: DNS queries, HTTP requests, flow data
  • Authentication: Logon events, privilege usage
  • Application: Web server logs, database queries

Step 3: Analyze

Look for patterns, anomalies, and indicators:

  • Statistical outliers (rare processes, unusual connections)
  • Known-bad indicators (IOCs)
  • Behavioral patterns (sequences of actions)

Step 4: Document Findings

Whether you find a threat or not, document:

  • The hypothesis tested
  • Data sources and queries used
  • Findings (true positive, false positive, or inconclusive)
  • Recommendations

Step 5: Improve Defenses

Turn hunts into detections:

  • Create automated alerts for confirmed TTPs
  • Update detection rules
  • Identify visibility gaps

2. Hypothesis Generation Sources

Where do good hunting hypotheses come from?

Threat Intelligence

  • APT reports: What TTPs are threat groups using?
  • Industry-specific threats: What's targeting your sector?
  • Emerging threats: New malware, zero-days, attack techniques

MITRE ATT&CK Framework

The ATT&CK framework is a threat hunter's best friend. It catalogs adversary tactics and techniques with real-world examples.

ATT&CK Tactics (Kill Chain):

Reconnaissance Initial Access Execution Persistence Privilege Escalation Defense Evasion Credential Access Discovery Lateral Movement Collection Exfiltration Command & Control

Environmental Knowledge

  • What's normal? Baseline behaviors for your environment
  • What's valuable? Crown jewels that attract attackers
  • What's vulnerable? Known weaknesses, unpatched systems

Past Incidents

  • How did past attackers get in?
  • What TTPs were used?
  • Are there similar patterns still occurring?

3. Hunting Techniques

Technique 1: IOC-Based Hunting

Search for known-bad indicators‚Äîhashes, IPs, domains, file names.

Hunt: Known Malware Hashes

Hypothesis: Malware from recent APT campaign may be present in our environment.

# Splunk - Search for known bad hashes
index=endpoint (hash=e99a18c428cb38d5f260853678922e03 OR 
               hash=5d41402abc4b2a76b9719d911017c592)
| table _time, host, file_path, hash, user

Pros: Quick, easy, specific
Cons: Only finds known threats; attackers can change IOCs easily

Technique 2: TTP-Based Hunting

Search for adversary behaviors rather than specific indicators.

Hunt: PowerShell Download Cradles

Hypothesis: Attackers may use PowerShell to download and execute payloads.

Execution T1059.001 - PowerShell

# Splunk - PowerShell download patterns
index=windows EventCode=4688 
    New_Process_Name="*powershell.exe*"
    (Process_Command_Line="*downloadstring*" OR
     Process_Command_Line="*downloadfile*" OR
     Process_Command_Line="*invoke-webrequest*" OR
     Process_Command_Line="*wget*" OR
     Process_Command_Line="*curl*" OR
     Process_Command_Line="*Net.WebClient*")
| table _time, host, user, Process_Command_Line
Hunt: Suspicious Parent-Child Process Relationships

Hypothesis: Malicious documents spawn unexpected child processes.

Execution T1204 - User Execution

# Splunk - Office spawning suspicious children
index=windows EventCode=4688
    (Parent_Process_Name="*WINWORD.EXE*" OR 
     Parent_Process_Name="*EXCEL.EXE*" OR
     Parent_Process_Name="*OUTLOOK.EXE*")
    (New_Process_Name="*cmd.exe*" OR
     New_Process_Name="*powershell.exe*" OR
     New_Process_Name="*wscript.exe*" OR
     New_Process_Name="*mshta.exe*" OR
     New_Process_Name="*certutil.exe*")
| table _time, host, user, Parent_Process_Name, New_Process_Name, Process_Command_Line

Technique 3: Anomaly-Based Hunting

Find statistical outliers‚Äîthings that deviate from normal.

Hunt: Rare Processes

Hypothesis: Malware often uses unusual process names that are rare in the environment.

# Splunk - Find rare processes
index=windows EventCode=4688
| stats count by New_Process_Name
| where count < 5
| sort count
| head 50
Hunt: Beaconing Detection

Hypothesis: C2 beacons maintain regular check-in intervals.

Command & Control T1071 - Application Layer Protocol

# Splunk - Detect regular connection intervals
index=network
| bin _time span=1m
| stats count by src_ip, dest_ip, _time
| streamstats window=60 stdev(count) as std, avg(count) as avg by src_ip, dest_ip
| where std < 2 AND count > 50  # Low variance = beaconing
| table src_ip, dest_ip, std, avg, count

Technique 4: Baseline Comparison

Compare current activity against known-good baselines.

Hunt: New Scheduled Tasks

Hypothesis: Attackers create scheduled tasks for persistence.

Persistence T1053.005 - Scheduled Task

# Splunk - New scheduled tasks in last 24h
index=windows EventCode=4698
| where _time > relative_time(now(), "-24h")
| table _time, host, TaskName, user, Task_Content
| outputlookup known_scheduled_tasks.csv

# Compare against baseline
| inputlookup known_scheduled_tasks_baseline.csv
| append [search index=windows EventCode=4698 | where _time > relative_time(now(), "-24h")]
| stats count by TaskName
| where count == 1  # New tasks only

4. High-Value Hunting Areas

Focus your hunts where attackers commonly operate:

Persistence Mechanisms

  • Scheduled Tasks: schtasks, Task Scheduler events
  • Services: New service creation (Event ID 7045)
  • Registry Run Keys: HKCU\Software\Microsoft\Windows\CurrentVersion\Run
  • Startup Folder: %APPDATA%\Microsoft\Windows\Start Menu\Programs\Startup
  • WMI Subscriptions: Permanent event consumers

Credential Access

  • LSASS Access: Processes reading LSASS memory (Mimikatz)
  • SAM/NTDS.dit Access: Credential database theft
  • Kerberoasting: Unusual TGS requests
  • DCSync: Replication requests from non-DCs

Lateral Movement

  • PsExec/SMB: Remote service creation, admin share access
  • WMI: Remote process creation via WMI
  • RDP: Unusual RDP connections, especially internal
  • Pass-the-Hash: Type 3 logons with NTLMv2

Data Exfiltration

  • Large Transfers: Unusual bytes out to external IPs
  • Cloud Storage: Connections to Dropbox, Google Drive, Mega
  • DNS Tunneling: Long DNS queries, high query volume
  • Archive Creation: RAR, ZIP creation in sensitive directories

5. Building a Hunting Program

Prerequisites

You can't hunt without visibility:

  • Log collection: Endpoint, network, authentication, application
  • Centralized SIEM: Searchable, fast, with sufficient retention
  • Baseline knowledge: What's normal in your environment
  • Threat intelligence: Current threat landscape awareness

  • MISP: Threat intel platform
  • OpenCTI: Open-source cyber threat intelligence

7. My Hunting Tips

Lessons from the Hunt:
  1. Start with what you know. Hunt in areas where you have good visibility.
  2. Document everything. Even "no findings" is valuable data.
  3. Think like an attacker. What would you do after getting initial access?
  4. Baseline first. You can't find anomalies without knowing normal.
  5. Automate your wins. Turn successful hunts into detection rules.
  6. Collaborate. Share findings with your team and the community.
  7. Don't get discouraged. Most hunts find nothing‚Äîthat's expected.
  8. Stay curious. The best hunters never stop learning.

Conclusion

Threat hunting is the evolution of security from passive defense to active pursuit. It requires deep technical skills, creative thinking, and relentless curiosity. Not every hunt will find a threat, but every hunt makes you a better defender.

The adversaries are already in your network. Go find them.
