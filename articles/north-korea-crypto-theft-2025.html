North Korea Stole $2.02 Billion in Cryptocurrency in 2025: The World's Leading State Sponsor of Crypto Theft

As 2025 draws to a close, North Korean state-sponsored hackers have cemented their position as the world's most prolific cryptocurrency thieves, stealing over $2 billion to fund weapons programs.

Year-End Report: 2025 Published: Dec 22, 2025 18 min read
$2.02 BILLION Cryptocurrency stolen by DPRK hackers in 2025
Sanctions Evasion at Scale: North Korea's cryptocurrency theft represents one of the largest sanctions evasion operations in history. The stolen funds directly support the regime's nuclear weapons and ballistic missile programs, making this a matter of global security.

Executive Summary

North Korean state-sponsored hacking groups, primarily operating under the umbrella known as Lazarus Group, have stolen approximately $2.02 billion in cryptocurrency during 2025. This staggering figure places North Korea as the undisputed global leader in nation-state cryptocurrency theft, outpacing all other threat actors combined.

The DPRK's crypto theft operations have evolved into a sophisticated, industrialized enterprise. From exchange hacks to DeFi protocol exploits to supply chain attacks targeting cryptocurrency developers, North Korean hackers have diversified their tactics while maintaining a relentless pace of operations.

Intelligence agencies and blockchain analysts estimate that cryptocurrency theft now funds a significant portion of North Korea's weapons programs, effectively turning the global crypto ecosystem into an unwitting financier of nuclear proliferation.

Attack Vectors and Tactics

North Korean hackers employ a diverse range of tactics to steal cryptocurrency:

1. Social Engineering

Perhaps the most effective tactic remains old-fashioned social engineering. DPRK operatives:

  • Create fake LinkedIn profiles posing as recruiters or investors
  • Target cryptocurrency developers with fake job offers
  • Send malicious documents disguised as job descriptions or investment opportunities
  • Build long-term relationships before deploying malware

2. Supply Chain Attacks

North Korea has increasingly targeted the software supply chain:

  • Compromising npm packages used by crypto projects
  • Injecting malicious code into open-source libraries
  • Targeting development tools and IDE plugins
  • Compromising CI/CD pipelines

3. DeFi Protocol Exploits

Decentralized finance protocols have proven particularly vulnerable:

  • Smart contract vulnerabilities
  • Flash loan attacks
  • Oracle manipulation
  • Bridge protocol exploits

4. Exchange Compromises

Traditional exchange hacks remain a profitable avenue:

  • Hot wallet compromises
  • Employee phishing
  • Infrastructure attacks
  • Insider recruitment
The IT Worker Scheme: North Korea also deploys thousands of IT workers abroad who use fake identities to secure remote work at tech and crypto companies. These insiders can provide access for larger attacks or simply funnel salaries back to the regime.

Major 2025 Incidents

While specific attribution is often delayed, several major 2025 cryptocurrency thefts are believed to be linked to North Korean actors:

Q1 2025 Multiple DeFi bridge exploits result in approximately $400 million in losses. Blockchain analysts trace funds through Tornado Cash and other mixing services to wallets associated with previous DPRK operations.
Q2 2025 A major cryptocurrency exchange suffers a hot wallet breach, losing over $300 million. Investigation reveals the attack began with a spear-phishing campaign targeting developers months earlier.
Q3 2025 Supply chain attack on popular npm package affects dozens of cryptocurrency projects. The compromised package includes code designed to steal private keys from developer machines.
Q4 2025 Year-end surge in activity as DPRK hackers rush to meet apparent quotas. Multiple smaller thefts aggregate to hundreds of millions in stolen funds.

The Laundering Pipeline

Stolen cryptocurrency doesn't immediately benefit the regime. It must first be laundered through a sophisticated pipeline:

  1. Initial Obfuscation: Funds are moved through hundreds of wallets rapidly
  2. Chain Hopping: Assets are bridged across multiple blockchains
  3. Mixing Services: Tornado Cash and other mixers obscure transaction trails
  4. OTC Brokers: Over-the-counter dealers in Asia convert crypto to fiat
  5. Shell Companies: Front companies receive "legitimate" payments
  6. Final Transfer: Funds ultimately reach North Korean-controlled accounts

Defending Against DPRK Attacks

Organizations in the cryptocurrency space should implement robust defenses:

Priority Defenses:
  • Security Awareness Training: Focus on social engineering and fake job offers
  • Hardware Wallets: Critical assets should never touch hot wallets
  • Multi-Signature Requirements: Major transactions require multiple approvals
  • Supply Chain Security: Audit all dependencies and use lockfiles
  • Background Checks: Verify employee identities, especially remote workers
  • Blockchain Monitoring: Watch for suspicious wallet activity

International Response

The international community has taken steps to combat North Korean crypto theft, but challenges remain:

Sanctions

  • Treasury Department OFAC sanctions on Lazarus Group and associated wallets
  • Sanctions on mixing services like Tornado Cash
  • Designations of individuals involved in laundering

Law Enforcement Actions

  • FBI disruption of DPRK-linked infrastructure
  • Seizure of cryptocurrency from identified wallets
  • International cooperation through Interpol and partner agencies

Challenges

  • North Korea's isolation limits traditional law enforcement options
  • Cryptocurrency's pseudonymous nature aids attackers
  • Mixing services and chain hopping complicate tracing
  • Decentralized protocols can't freeze stolen funds

Looking Ahead to 2026

Based on current trends, we expect North Korean cryptocurrency theft to continue escalating:

  • AI-Enhanced Attacks: Use of AI for more convincing social engineering
  • Larger Targets: Focus on bigger exchanges and DeFi protocols
  • New Chains: Expansion to emerging blockchain ecosystems
  • Supply Chain Focus: More sophisticated developer targeting
  • Insider Threats: Continued infiltration of IT workforces
