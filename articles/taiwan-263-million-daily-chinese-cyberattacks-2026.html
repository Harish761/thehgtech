Taiwan Reports 2.63 Million Daily Chinese Cyberattacks: Energy Infrastructure Under Siege

Taiwan's National Security Bureau reveals an unprecedented scale of Chinese cyber operations in 2025—averaging 2.63 million attacks per day. Energy infrastructure attacks increased tenfold. Welcome to the digital front of cross-strait conflict.

Report: January 3, 2026 Published: Jan 5, 2026 20 min read By Harish G
2,630,000 Cyberattacks Per Day from China Average recorded by Taiwan's National Security Bureau in 2025
REPORT FINDINGS: Taiwan's National Security Bureau (NSB) has released its annual cyber threat assessment, revealing that Chinese cyberattacks against Taiwan averaged 2.63 million incidents per day throughout 2025. Most alarming: attacks on critical energy infrastructure increased by a factor of 10 compared to the previous year.

The Digital Front Line

While the world watches Taiwan Strait tensions through the lens of military exercises and diplomatic rhetoric, a far more immediate conflict rages in cyberspace. Taiwan has become the most heavily targeted territory on Earth for nation-state cyberattacks, and the numbers released by the NSB paint a picture of relentless digital siege.

To put 2.63 million daily attacks in perspective:

  • 30 attacks per second — Every second of every day
  • 960 million attacks per year — Nearly a billion cyber incidents
  • 40 attacks per citizen per year — If distributed across Taiwan's 24 million population

These aren't just automated scans. The NSB report emphasizes that many attacks involve sophisticated, targeted operations against specific government agencies, critical infrastructure, and strategic industries.

Energy Infrastructure: The Primary Target

The most striking finding in the NSB report is the tenfold increase in attacks targeting Taiwan's energy sector. This shift reveals Beijing's strategic priorities in any potential cross-strait conflict scenario.

Why Energy?

Taiwan's energy dependency is its Achilles' heel:

  • 97.8% energy import dependent — Taiwan imports nearly all fossil fuels
  • Island isolation — No interconnected grid with other nations
  • Concentrated infrastructure — Major power plants and LNG terminals are few and critical
  • Industrial dependency — TSMC and semiconductor fabs require stable power
Strategic Implication: A successful cyberattack that disrupts Taiwan's power grid for even 48 hours could halt semiconductor production, affecting global supply chains. For context, TSMC alone manufactures over 90% of the world's most advanced chips.

Attack Patterns and Tactics

The NSB report identifies several attack categories and their relative frequencies:

1. Reconnaissance and Probing (45%)

Continuous scanning of Taiwan's networks to map infrastructure, identify vulnerabilities, and maintain updated attack blueprints. This "preparation" activity accounts for nearly half of all recorded incidents.

2. Credential Theft and Phishing (25%)

Targeted spear-phishing campaigns against government officials, military personnel, and critical infrastructure employees. The goal: obtain legitimate credentials for deeper penetration.

3. Malware Deployment (15%)

Installation of backdoors, remote access trojans (RATs), and pre-positioned payloads that can be activated during a conflict. Many of these lay dormant for months or years.

4. Data Exfiltration (10%)

Active theft of classified information, military plans, industrial secrets, and personal data of key officials for intelligence purposes or future coercion.

5. Destructive Operations (5%)

Actual attacks intended to disrupt services—though likely held in reserve for potential conflict scenarios.

Pre-positioning Strategy: Security researchers believe China is "pre-positioning" access to critical systems—maintaining persistent backdoors that can be activated to cause maximum disruption during any military confrontation. This mirrors Russian tactics observed in Ukraine before the 2022 invasion.

Geopolitical Context: Why Now?

The escalation in cyberattacks tracks closely with rising cross-strait tensions:

2024-2025 Flashpoints

  • January 2024: Taiwan presidential election won by Lai Ching-te (DPP), viewed unfavorably by Beijing
  • May 2024: Inauguration prompts large-scale Chinese military exercises
  • Throughout 2025: Increased PLA Air Force incursions into Taiwan's ADIZ
  • Late 2025: US-Taiwan arms sales announcements trigger Chinese countermeasures

Cyber operations provide China with a below-threshold response mechanism—aggressive enough to punish Taiwan and demonstrate capability, but not escalatory enough to trigger direct military confrontation with the United States.

Taiwan's Defensive Posture

Taiwan has significantly enhanced its cyber defenses in response:

Organizational Improvements

  • National Institute of Cyber Security (NICS): Expanded mandate and budget
  • Military Cyber Command: Integrated with overall defense planning
  • Public-private partnerships: Critical infrastructure operators required to share threat intelligence

Technical Measures

  • Zero Trust Architecture: Implemented across government networks
  • AI-based threat detection: Machine learning systems for anomaly detection
  • Backup systems: Offline capabilities for essential services
  • International cooperation: Information sharing with US, Japan, and other allies

Societal Resilience

  • Cyber drills: Regular exercises involving public and private sectors
  • Disinformation defense: Fact-checking initiatives and media literacy programs
  • Reserve cyber force: Training civilians with IT skills for national defense
Silver Lining: Taiwan's daily exposure to sophisticated cyberattacks has made it one of the most cyber-resilient societies in the world. Organizations being attacked 2.63 million times daily either adapt or fail—Taiwan has adapted.

Lessons for the Rest of the World

Taiwan's experience offers critical lessons for other nations facing state-sponsored cyber threats:

1. Critical Infrastructure is the Prize

Energy, water, telecommunications—these are the targets that matter most in a conflict scenario. Protecting them requires dedicated resources and cross-sector coordination.

2. Volume Matters

High-volume attacks serve multiple purposes: reconnaissance, distraction, resource exhaustion, and concealment of more targeted operations. Defenders must automate to cope.

3. Pre-positioning is the New Normal

Adversaries don't just attack when they need to—they establish access years in advance. Threat hunting for dormant implants is essential.

4. Resilience Over Prevention

At 2.63 million attacks per day, some will inevitably succeed. The focus must be on detection, response, and recovery—not just prevention.

5. International Cooperation is Essential

No nation can face state-sponsored cyber threats alone. Intelligence sharing, joint exercises, and coordinated responses multiply defensive capabilities.

What to Watch

As cross-strait tensions continue, several indicators will signal escalation or de-escalation in the cyber domain:

  • Attack volume spikes: Significant increases around political events or military exercises
  • New APT activity: Emergence of previously unknown threat groups
  • Destructive attacks: Shift from espionage to sabotage would signal intent change
  • Critical infrastructure incidents: Actual disruptions to power, water, or communications
  • Cross-border coordination: Attacks timed with operations against allies (Japan, Philippines)

Key Takeaways

Summary Points:
  • 2.63 million daily attacks: Taiwan is the most cyber-targeted territory on Earth
  • 10x energy sector increase: Critical infrastructure is the primary focus
  • Pre-positioning strategy: China is establishing access for potential future conflict
  • Multiple APT groups: APT41, APT40, Volt Typhoon, and others actively involved
  • Resilience is key: Taiwan's response offers lessons for global defenders

Implications for 2026 and Beyond

The NSB report confirms what security researchers have long suspected: China is conducting the most sustained cyber campaign in history against a single target. Whether this remains below the threshold of armed conflict depends on factors far beyond cyberspace.

For security professionals worldwide, Taiwan serves as both a warning and a model. A warning of what state-sponsored cyber warfare looks like at scale—and a model of how determined defenders can maintain resilience against overwhelming odds.

The 2.63 million daily attacks aren't just Taiwan's problem. In an interconnected world where supply chains span continents and semiconductor production concentration creates global dependencies, Taiwan's cyber security is everyone's concern.
