The Wrapper Epidemic: Behind the Curtain of the India AI Summit 2026

The Bharat Mandapam was meant to showcase India’s emergence as a sovereign AI superpower. Instead, it became a battleground of "borrowed" technology, exposing the dangerous habit of slapping native labels on Silicon Valley APIs, while genuine indigenous brilliance fought to be seen.

NEW DELHI — The Bharat Mandapam, a colossal architectural marvel built for the G20, was pulsing with the electric hum of the future. From February 16th through the 21st, it hosted the India AI Impact Summit 2026. Billed heavily by the IndiaAI Mission and the Ministry of Electronics & IT, the goal was audacious but vital: to cement the Global South’s definitive voice in the artificial intelligence arms race.

The overtures were grand. Over thirty countries participated. Three hundred exhibitors packed the vast halls. In a stunning display of organizational mobilization, India set a new Guinness World Record, securing an astonishing 250,946 pledges for an AI responsibility campaign within a single 24-hour window. The official narrative pumped out to global wire services was one of total triumph—a flawless execution of the summit’s "People, Planet, and Progress" mantra.

But that was the press release. If you spent any meaningful time walking the crowded floor of the India AI Impact Expo, talking to the exhausted engineers rather than the polished PR executives, a much darker narrative was compiling in real-time.

Behind the colossal, neon-lit booths of the American tech oligarchy, the summit exposed a localized crisis: the systemic repackaging of foreign intellectual property masquerading as indigenous innovation. It was a summit where the promise of a sovereign AI ecosystem collided violently with the reality of VC-funded "vaporware"—and where the true, brilliant architects of India’s technological future found themselves fighting not just geopolitical rivals, but their own heavily funded countrymen.

"The promise of 'AI for All' quickly devolved into 'AI for the Best UI Wrapper.' We are handing out unicorn valuations to companies essentially operating as glorified API tollbooths."

The Hardware Embarrassment: The Galgotias Incident

To understand the software crisis, one must look at how it manifested in physical form right on the expo floor. The pressure to present something—anything—that looked like homegrown deep-tech innovation pushed some participants past the brink of hyperbole and straight into outright fabrication.

One of the most widely discussed friction points occurred at the stall belonging to Galgotias University. In what was meant to be a showcase of advanced robotics and engineering, the university proudly presented "Orion"—a sleek, quadrupedal robotic dog capable of navigating complex terrain and performing acrobatic maneuvers. To a layperson, it looked like a stunning achievement of university-backed electromechanical engineering.

But the cybersecurity and robotics community on X (formerly Twitter) and LinkedIn operates with ruthless efficiency. Within hours of photos circulating from the summit floor, eagle-eyed technologists, forensic analysts, and hardware enthusiasts rapidly demystified "Orion."

It wasn't an indigenous marvel designed in Noida.

It was a Unitree Go2—a highly advanced, totally commercially available robotic dog manufactured by Unitree Robotics, a firm based in Hangzhou, China. It is a product you can literally add to an online shopping cart for a few thousand dollars.

Investigative Note: Hardware White-Labeling

The practice of "white-labeling" hardware—buying a manufactured product, removing the original badging, and applying one's own branding—is common in consumer electronics (like cheap Bluetooth headphones). Applying this strategy to deep-tech robotics at a governmental summit celebrating "Indigenous Innovation" crosses the line from marketing to deception.

The backlash was instant and severe. The brazenness of resting a "Make in India" claim on an off-the-shelf Chinese import was seen as a profound embarrassment to the genuine hardware startups present. Reports rapidly surfaced that organizers, under immense pressure, asked Galgotias to vacate their stall. The controversy even violently spilled over into the political arena, prompting heated inquiries within the Uttar Pradesh Assembly regarding the allocation of funds and the vetting of technical claims.

The university eventually issued a frantic retraction and apology, pinning the catastrophic PR failure on an "ill-informed" representative who had allegedly spoken to the press without authorization. But the damage to the collective ethos of the expo was done.

The "Galgotias Syndrome," however, was merely a physical, easily photographable symptom of a much deeper, more insidious disease running through the Indian tech ecosystem. The software equivalent is far harder to spot, and involves billions of dollars in venture capital.

The Software Epidemic: The Krutrim Controversy

If purchasing a Chinese robot and renaming it is an obvious foul, creating an "indigenous language model" by secretly querying servers in California is the sophisticated tech-bro equivalent. This is the Wrapper Epidemic.

The controversy surrounding Ola’s Krutrim AI loomed heavily over the discussions in the Summit's coffee lines and VIP lounges. Billed aggressively as "India's own AI" and catapulted rapidly to Unicorn status (a valuation over $1 billion), Krutrim was meant to be the poster child for sovereign capability.

But when users began stress-testing the public interface, the facade cracked spectacularly. When pointedly asked, "Who created you?", the chatbot didn't return an answer citing Ola's brilliant engineers. It responded, plainly and unequivocally, that it was developed by OpenAI.

The tech community exploded with allegations that Krutrim was nothing more than a superficial UI "wrapper" built around ChatGPT's API endpoints. A wrapper is exactly what it sounds like: a thin layer of code that takes a user's prompt, secretly hands it off to a third-party massive corporation (like Anthropic or OpenAI), waits for the answer, and then serves it back to the user with a customized logo strapped to the top.

The Anatomy of a "Wrapper"

1. User Inputs Prompt to "Sovereign AI"

2. The "Wrapper" Startup (Zero compute cost, thin UI layer)

3. Hidden API Call to OpenAI / Anthropic (California)

4. Response Stamped with "Made in India" Logo

For their part, Ola's leadership vehemently denied operating a simple wrapper. They published statements blaming a "data leakage issue" originating from an open-source dataset used during the model's fine-tuning phase. Essentially, they argued that they had trained their own model from scratch, but had accidentally fed it so much synthetic data generated by ChatGPT that the model began hallucinating OpenAI's corporate identity as its own.

While technically plausible in the arcane world of LLM fine-tuning, the explanation did little to quell the underlying anxiety. Beyond the attribution error, users reported that the model lacked vast swathes of nuance, tripped over fundamental logical queries, and aggressively avoided controversial subjects through heavy-handed term-of-service blocking.

The questions whispered at the summit were brutal: Are we granting billion-dollar valuations to companies essentially running a cURL request to San Francisco? When we say we are building "Sovereign AI," are we building the foundational compute architecture, the immense data pipelines, and the tensor-optimized clusters? Or are we just building the frontend HTML interface?

The Genuine Vanguard: The Models That Mattered

The absolute greatest tragedy of the wrapper controversies and the hardware fiascos is that they steal the oxygen from the developers who are actually doing the excruciating, unglamorous, foundational work. True sovereign AI does exist in India, and it was present at the summit, fighting through the noise.

These are the entities not reliant on massive API budgets, but rather on profound architectural innovation designed specifically for the unique constraints of the subcontinent.

1. Bhashini: The Public Infrastructure Triumphs

Perhaps the most critical initiative in the Global South right now is the government-backed Bhashini project. Rather than trying to build a conversational omni-bot to write marketing copy, Bhashini is focused on a fundamental human right: access to information.

India consists of 22 official languages and hundreds of dialects. Bhashini is quietly building massive open-source language datasets and localized models to break down these barriers. They are creating the foundational data architecture that allows rural farmers, who may not speak English or standard Hindi, to interact with the digital economy via natural voice. It is highly optimized, socially critical, and authentically local.

2. Sarvam AI (OpenHathi): True Foundational Architecture

Unlike startups allegedly proxying requests to the West, firms like Sarvam AI are doing the heavy lifting of extending foundational architectures. Their work on things like OpenHathi—a series of LLMs specifically modeled and heavily fine-tuned for Hindi—proves that India can build and modify core architecture.

By heavily adjusting the tokenization processes (how an AI breaks down words), they have made running Indic-language AI vastly more efficient, reducing the immense compute costs that normally plague non-English model operations.

3. KissanAI: The Power of the Edge

The Silicon Valley playbook is simple: build models so massive they require $50 million supercomputer clusters to answer a single question, and then charge a subscription. The Indian reality requires a different approach: Edge Computing.

Innovations like KissanAI represent a deeply practical approach. It is an LLM designed specifically for rural agriculture, providing localized farming advisories via voice. But the genius isn't just the data; it's the optimization. These are Small Language Models (SLMs) designed to run efficiently in low-bandwidth, low-compute environments. They don't need a fiber-optic connection to California to tell a farmer in Punjab how to identify crop blight. That is what true technological sovereignty looks like.

"We do not achieve technological independence by paying API taxes to Menlo Park. We achieve it by building Small Language Models that can run on a $150 smartphone in an area with a 3G connection."

The Dark Comedy of the Main Stage

While the battles over wrappers and open-source models raged in the expo halls, the global media largely ignored the technical nuance in favor of elite spectacle. And on day three, the most powerful men in technology inadvertently delivered a masterclass in corporate friction.

During a heavily promoted photo-op intended to project a united, global front on AI safety, Prime Minister Narendra Modi stood center stage. To his sides were the titans: Google’s Sundar Pichai, DeepMind’s Demis Hassabis, Anthropic’s Dario Amodei, and OpenAI’s Sam Altman.

The intent was clear: a unified, overhead hand-raise. A classic symbol of collective human endeavor.

It was a catastrophic physical failure. Sam Altman and Dario Amodei—whose companies are locked in a vicious, existential battle for multi-billion-dollar enterprise contracts and compute supremacy—appeared visibly panicked by the prospect of physical contact. What followed was five seconds of excruciatingly awkward fumbling, hesitant half-grabs, and confused glances, before the collective eventually, awkwardly, thrust a disjointed series of individual closed fists into the air.

The clip instantly went viral, flooding Reddit and X with memes. But beyond the dark comedy of tech billionaires unable to coordinate basic motor functions, the moment was profoundly symbolic.

It summarized the exact reality of the AI industry. These corporations are fiercely competitive silos. They are utterly incapable of genuine collaboration when intellectual property and market dominance are on the line. Expecting these entities to hold hands and cooperatively guide the ethical future of humanity is akin to expecting oil barons to cooperatively plan the transition to solar energy.

The Battleground Ahead

The India AI Impact Summit 2026 successfully proved a thesis that Silicon Valley has known for two years: India is no longer just a passive consumer market or an IT outsourcing hub. It is the definitive battleground for the future of artificial intelligence deployability.

But the summit also laid bare the profound risks of the current trajectory. The allure of quick venture capital has incentivized a fast-food approach to machine learning—slapping a localized UI over foreign infrastructure and selling it as high-tech gourmet.

As the lights power down at the Bharat Mandapam, the true test begins. We must choose whether we will fund the painstaking, capital-intensive architectures required to build the next Sarvam AI, or if we will continue to buy plastic robotic dogs and heavily marked-up API keys for the exact same CEOs who can't even stand to hold hands in front of the cameras.

The age of the wrapper must end. The era of the foundational builder must begin.
