MuddyWater Infiltrates US Banks and Airports with Novel Backdoors After Iran Strikes

Iran's Ministry of Intelligence-linked APT group MuddyWater (Seedworm) has compromised networks of US banks, airports, and defense contractors since early February 2026. Broadcom's Symantec reveals two previously unknown backdoors — Dindoor, built on the Deno JavaScript runtime, and Fakeset, a Python-based implant — in what appears to be retaliatory cyber operations following US and Israeli military strikes on Iran.

March 9, 2026 18 min read Harish G APT / Nation-State
EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
  • Threat Actor: MuddyWater (Seedworm / Temp.Zagros / Static Kitten) — MOIS-linked Iranian APT
  • Targets: US bank, US airport, Canadian non-profit, Israeli defense software company
  • Timeframe: Active since early February 2026, intensifying after late February military strikes on Iran
  • New Tools: Dindoor (Deno JS backdoor) and Fakeset (Python backdoor) — both previously unknown
  • Objective: Espionage and data exfiltration
  • Attribution Confidence: High — based on code-signing certificates, infrastructure overlap, and historical TTPs
  • Discovered by: Broadcom Symantec Threat Hunter Team and Carbon Black

Who Is MuddyWater?

MuddyWater Seedworm Temp.Zagros Static Kitten
Affiliation: Iran's Ministry of Intelligence and Security (MOIS)
Active Since: 2017
Primary Objective: Espionage, intelligence gathering
Typical Targets: Government, telecommunications, energy, defense, academia
Attribution: Formally attributed by USCYBERCOM, CISA, and FBI in January 2022
Known Tools: PowGoop, POWERSTATS, Stagecomp, Darkcomp, and now Dindoor + Fakeset

MuddyWater is not a hacktivist group or a criminal gang. It is a formal cyber operations unit within Iran's Ministry of Intelligence and Security — the Iranian equivalent of the CIA. In January 2022, US Cyber Command (USCYBERCOM), CISA, and the FBI formally attributed MuddyWater to MOIS and published technical indicators.

The group has operated for nearly a decade, primarily targeting Middle Eastern governments, telecoms, and energy companies. But the February 2026 campaign marks a significant escalation: direct targeting of US critical infrastructure — banks, airports, and defense supply chain companies.

The Targets

According to Symantec's Threat Hunter Team, MuddyWater has compromised or attempted to compromise at least four organizations:

Critical Context: The targeting of a US bank and a US airport represents a significant escalation in MuddyWater's operational scope. Iran's cyber operations have historically focused on the Middle East. Direct targeting of US financial and aviation infrastructure signals that the geopolitical conflict has entered a new cyber dimension.

New Backdoor #1: Dindoor (Deno-Based)

Dindoor is a newly discovered backdoor that leverages Deno — a modern, secure runtime for JavaScript and TypeScript created by Node.js creator Ryan Dahl. This represents a novel "Bring Your Own Runtime" (BYOR) evasion strategy.

Why Deno?

Most EDR solutions are configured to monitor common runtimes and interpreters — PowerShell, Python, cmd.exe, bash. Deno is relatively obscure in enterprise environments, which gives MuddyWater several advantages:

  • Low detection rate: EDR rules rarely trigger on Deno process execution
  • Legitimate binary: Deno is a legitimate, signed developer tool — it doesn't trip reputation-based detection
  • Cross-platform: Deno runs on Windows, macOS, and Linux
  • Network-capable: Deno has built-in HTTP client and can execute TypeScript directly from remote URLs

Dindoor Attribution Evidence

Dindoor was digitally signed with a certificate issued to an individual named "Amy Cherne". While the name itself is likely fictitious, the certificate provides a link to MuddyWater's infrastructure and operational patterns.

New Backdoor #2: Fakeset (Python-Based)

Fakeset is a Python-based backdoor that was primarily found on the network of the targeted US airport. Key characteristics:

  • Language: Python — common in enterprise environments, making it harder to distinguish from legitimate scripts
  • Signed certificates: Signed with certificates issued to both "Amy Cherne" and "Donald Gay"
  • Hosting: Downloaded from legitimate cloud storage — specifically Backblaze servers — abusing trusted platforms for staging
  • Historical link: The "Donald Gay" certificate has been previously associated with other Seedworm malware families including Stagecomp and Darkcomp — strengthening the attribution
Attribution Chain: The "Donald Gay" code-signing certificate is the strongest link to MuddyWater. This same certificate was used to sign Stagecomp and Darkcomp — tools previously attributed to Seedworm/MuddyWater by multiple security research teams (Symantec, Palo Alto Unit 42, Cisco Talos). The reuse of signing infrastructure is a common OPSEC failure among nation-state groups.

Geopolitical Context: Why Now?

The timing of this campaign is directly linked to the escalating conflict between the United States, Israel, and Iran:

  • Late February 2026: US and Israeli military strikes targeted Iranian military infrastructure
  • Iran's "Great Epic" campaign: Iranian-aligned threat groups launched retaliatory cyber operations targeting fuel infrastructure in Jordan, ICS systems in Israel, and US military logistics providers
  • MuddyWater activation: Symantec observed MuddyWater's intrusions beginning in early February 2026 — suggesting the operation was pre-planned, not reactive. The strikes may have triggered escalation, but the groundwork was already laid
  • Palo Alto Unit 42: Published a separate threat brief highlighting "significant increase in Iranian cyber risk" for March 2026

The targeting pattern tells a story: a US bank (financial intelligence), a US airport (infrastructure mapping), and an Israeli defense software company (military supply chain). These are the intelligence targets you would prioritize if you were planning retaliatory operations or preparing for further military action.

Data Exfiltration: What They Took

Symantec's research confirms that MuddyWater attempted to exfiltrate data from the Israeli defense software company using Rclone — an open-source command-line tool for syncing files to cloud storage. The data was being transferred to a Wasabi cloud storage bucket.

Detection: Hunting for Dindoor and Fakeset

Hunt Query: Deno Process Execution (Anomalous)

# Linux/macOS — Hunt for unexpected Deno execution
# Deno is rarely used in enterprise environments
# Any execution outside developer workstations is suspicious

ps aux | grep -i deno
find / -name "deno" -type f 2>/dev/null
# Check for Deno in unexpected locations:
# /tmp/, /var/tmp/, AppData\Local\Temp\

# Windows — Check for Deno in process logs
# Event ID 4688 (Process Creation) with deno.exe
# PowerShell:
Get-WinEvent -FilterHashtable @{
    LogName='Security'; Id=4688
} | Where-Object {
    $_.Properties[5].Value -like '*deno*'
} | Select-Object TimeCreated, @{
    N='CommandLine'; E={$_.Properties[8].Value}
}

IOC Hunt: Code-Signing Certificates

# Hunt for binaries signed by known MuddyWater certificates
# Certificate subjects associated with this campaign:
#   - "Amy Cherne"
#   - "Donald Gay"

# Windows — Check Authenticode signatures
Get-ChildItem -Path C:\ -Recurse -Include *.exe,*.dll -ErrorAction SilentlyContinue |
    Get-AuthenticodeSignature |
    Where-Object { $_.SignerCertificate.Subject -match "Amy Cherne|Donald Gay" }

# Linux — Check for signed Python scripts from Backblaze
grep -r "backblaze\|wasabisys\|b2.backblazeb2" /var/log/ 2>/dev/null

Mitigations and Recommendations

RECOMMENDED ACTIONS:
  1. Hunt for Deno in your environment. If no developers use Deno, any deno.exe or deno binary execution should be investigated immediately
  2. Block Rclone. If your organization doesn't use Rclone for legitimate purposes, block or alert on its execution via application control policies
  3. Monitor cloud storage connections. Alert on connections to Wasabi (s3.wasabisys.com) and Backblaze (b2.backblazeb2.com) from non-standard processes
  4. Certificate monitoring. Add "Amy Cherne" and "Donald Gay" certificate subjects to your code-signing block list
  5. Review CISA MuddyWater advisories. Apply all mitigations from CISA's January 2022 advisory (AA22-055A) and subsequent updates
  6. Network segmentation. Ensure financial systems, SCADA/ICS, and airport operations networks are segmented from corporate IT
  7. Incident response readiness. If you operate in financial services, aviation, or defense — brief your IR team on MuddyWater TTPs immediately

Our Analysis

Three observations on this campaign:

  1. The BYOR technique is the real story. MuddyWater's use of Deno as a "Bring Your Own Runtime" is a significant tactical innovation. We're likely to see other APT groups adopt this technique — using legitimate developer runtimes (Bun, Deno, Go) as execution environments to bypass EDR detections calibrated for PowerShell and Python. Security teams need to inventory all runtime environments in their networks and create detection rules for anomalous usage.
  2. This is pre-positioned access, not reactive hacking. The intrusions began in early February — before the late February military strikes. This suggests MuddyWater was establishing access for intelligence collection or future operations, not retaliating. The strikes likely accelerated their timeline, not initiated it.
  3. US critical infrastructure targeting is the new normal. Iran, Russia, and China are all now actively targeting US banks, energy, water, and transportation infrastructure. The distinction between "espionage" and "pre-positioning for destructive attack" is increasingly academic. If they have access to an airport network, the intent matters less than the capability.
