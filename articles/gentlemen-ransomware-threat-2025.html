The Technical Arsenal

What makes Gentlemen particularly dangerous is their combination of techniques. Let's break down what they're working with:

BYOVD - Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver

This is the big one. The attackers deploy legitimate but vulnerable signed drivers onto victim systems. Because these drivers are properly signed by trusted vendors, most security tools let them through. Once loaded, these drivers are exploited to disable EDR agents, antivirus software, and other security controls. Your expensive endpoint protection? Neutralized before the encryption even starts.

GPO Manipulation

Instead of manually spreading through the network, Gentlemen leverages Active Directory Group Policy Objects to deploy their ransomware at scale. One compromised domain controller, and suddenly every machine in your environment is executing their payload on the next policy refresh. It's efficient, it's fast, and it's devastating.

Double Extortion Model

Before encrypting anything, Gentlemen exfiltrates your sensitive data. This means even if you have perfect backups and can restore without paying, they still have leverage. "Pay us, or we publish your customer data, intellectual property, and financial records." It's a business model that works, unfortunately.

Why I'm Particularly Concerned About This One

I've seen a lot of ransomware variants over the years. Most follow predictable patterns. But Gentlemen concerns me for several reasons:

The security industry has known about vulnerable drivers for years, but we still haven't solved the problem. Microsoft's HVCI and driver blocklist help, but they're not universally deployed. Most organizations I've worked with don't have driver allowlisting policies. Until we collectively address this gap, BYOVD attacks will remain highly effective.

Defending Against Gentlemen (and Similar Threats)

So what can you actually do? Here's my practical guidance based on their known TTPs:

Immediate Actions

  • Review your driver policies: Enable HVCI (Hypervisor-Protected Code Integrity) where possible. Deploy Microsoft's recommended driver blocklist. Consider driver allowlisting for critical systems.
  • Harden Active Directory: Audit GPO creation and modification rights. Implement tiered administration. Monitor for suspicious policy changes.
  • Protect your EDR: Enable tamper protection features. Ensure your EDR can detect driver-based attacks. Test whether vulnerable drivers can be loaded in your environment.

Detection Opportunities

  • Driver loading events: Monitor for unusual kernel driver installations, especially from temp directories or unusual paths
  • GPO modifications: Alert on Group Policy Object changes outside of approved change windows
  • Large data transfers: The exfiltration phase is your window to catch them before encryptionâ€”monitor for unusual outbound data volumes
  • EDR agent health: If your endpoint agents suddenly stop reporting, that's a major red flag
