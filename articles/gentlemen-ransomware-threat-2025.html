The Technical Arsenal: A Deep Dive

What makes Gentlemen particularly dangerous is their combination of techniques. Each one is effective on its own, but together they create a formidable attack chain that's difficult to detect and even harder to stop once it's in motion. Let's break down what they're working with:

BYOVD - Bring Your Own Vulnerable Driver

This is the cornerstone of their attack chain. The attackers deploy legitimate but vulnerable signed drivers onto victim systems. Because these drivers are properly signed by trusted vendors like Gigabyte, MSI, or ASUS, most security tools let them through without question.

Once loaded into the kernel (Ring 0), these vulnerable drivers are exploited to terminate EDR agents, antivirus processes, and backup services. Your expensive CrowdStrike or SentinelOne deployment? Neutralized before the encryption even starts.

How BYOVD Works: Step by Step

Understanding the BYOVD attack chain is crucial for defenders. Here's exactly how Gentlemen executes this technique:

  1. Driver Deployment: The attackers drop a legitimate but vulnerable signed driver (commonly gdrv.sys from Gigabyte or AsIO.sys from ASUS) to the target system. Because these drivers are properly signed by trusted hardware vendors, Windows allows them to load.
  2. Kernel-Level Access: The vulnerable driver contains flaws that allow arbitrary read/write to kernel memory. This gives the attacker Ring 0 (kernel) privileges‚Äîthe highest level of access on a Windows system.
  3. Security Tool Termination: Using kernel-level access, Gentlemen systematically terminates security processes by patching their memory, killing threads, or unloading their drivers. Even "tamper-protected" EDR solutions can be defeated at the kernel level.
  4. Ransomware Deployment: With security controls disabled, the ransomware payload executes without interference, encrypting files and exfiltrating data.
# Example vulnerable drivers commonly used in BYOVD attacks: gdrv.sys (Gigabyte) - CVE-2018-19320 AsIO.sys (ASUS) - Arbitrary read/write dbutil_2_3.sys (Dell) - CVE-2021-21551 RTCore64.sys (MSI) - CVE-2019-16098 WinRing0x64.sys - Multiple hardware vendors

Why is this so effective? Traditional security tools operate in user mode (Ring 3) while these vulnerable drivers operate in kernel mode (Ring 0). It's like trying to fight someone who has the high ground‚Äîyou're at a fundamental disadvantage.

GPO Manipulation for Mass Deployment

Instead of manually spreading through the network machine by machine (which is slow and increases detection risk), Gentlemen leverages Active Directory Group Policy Objects to deploy their ransomware at scale.

Once they compromise a domain controller‚Äîor even just an account with GPO modification rights‚Äîthey create or modify a Group Policy that executes their payload on every domain-joined machine during the next policy refresh. One click, thousands of encrypted endpoints.

The GPO Attack Chain

The abuse of Group Policy is elegant in its simplicity:

  1. Domain Compromise: After initial access (often via phishing or VPN credential theft), attackers escalate privileges until they have Domain Admin or equivalent rights.
  2. GPO Creation/Modification: A new GPO is created or an existing one is modified to include a startup script or scheduled task that executes the ransomware binary.
  3. Staging the Payload: The ransomware binary is placed in SYSVOL (which replicates to all domain controllers) or a network share accessible by all machines.
  4. Mass Execution: On the next Group Policy refresh (by default, every 90 minutes for computers), every domain-joined machine executes the ransomware. Alternatively, attackers can force an immediate refresh using gpupdate /force or invoke-gpupdate via PowerShell.
# PowerShell: Force GPO update on all domain computers Get-ADComputer -Filter * | ForEach-Object { Invoke-Command -ComputerName $_.Name -ScriptBlock { gpupdate /force } } # This single command can trigger ransomware on every machine in minutes

Double Extortion Model

Before encrypting anything, Gentlemen exfiltrates your sensitive data to their infrastructure. This means even if you have perfect backups and can restore without paying a dime, they still have leverage.

"Pay us, or we publish your customer data, intellectual property, financial records, and employee information." It's a business model that works, unfortunately. And with regulatory fines for data breaches often exceeding ransom demands, many organizations calculate that paying is the cheaper option.

Exfiltration Techniques

Gentlemen's data exfiltration is designed to evade detection:

  • Cloud Storage Abuse: Uploading to legitimate services like Mega, pCloud, or even Azure Blob storage to blend with normal traffic
  • Slow and Steady: Throttling transfer speeds to avoid triggering DLP alerts or bandwidth anomalies
  • Compression and Encryption: Data is compressed and encrypted before transfer, making content inspection impossible
  • Off-Hours Timing: Exfiltration often occurs during nights and weekends when monitoring is reduced

Why Gentlemen Concerns Me More Than Most

I've analyzed dozens of ransomware variants over the years. Most follow predictable patterns and exploitable weaknesses. But Gentlemen concerns me for several specific reasons:

1. The BYOVD Problem Is Getting Worse, Not Better

The security industry has known about vulnerable drivers for years. Microsoft maintains a driver blocklist. HVCI (Hypervisor-Protected Code Integrity) can prevent many BYOVD attacks. But here's the uncomfortable truth: most organizations haven't implemented these controls.

In my experience consulting with enterprises, I'd estimate fewer than 20% have HVCI enabled, and even fewer maintain updated driver blocklists. The gap between what's possible and what's deployed is vast‚Äî and Gentlemen is exploiting that gap ruthlessly.

2. They're Targeting Diverse Industries

Unlike ransomware groups that focus on healthcare (for urgency) or law firms (for confidentiality), Gentlemen appears to be opportunistic. The 17-country spread suggests they're hitting whoever they can compromise, whenever they can compromise them.

Reported victim industries include:

  • Manufacturing and industrial
  • Professional services (accounting, legal)
  • Technology and software
  • Healthcare (predictably)
  • Financial services
  • Education and research

If you're connected to the internet and have data worth stealing, you're in scope.

3. The Go Language Choice Is Strategic

Writing ransomware in Go (Golang) provides several operational advantages that make Gentlemen harder to detect:

  • Cross-Platform Compilation: A single codebase can target Windows, Linux, and macOS with minimal changes
  • Static Compilation: Go binaries are self-contained, reducing dependencies and making deployment simpler
  • Detection Evasion: Many security tools have less mature detection signatures for Go binaries compared to traditional C/C++ malware
  • Reverse Engineering Difficulty: Go binaries are notoriously difficult to reverse engineer, slowing down security research

4. Professional Operations Indicate Resources

The sophistication of Gentlemen's operations suggests this isn't a few hackers in a basement. They have:

  • Dedicated infrastructure for command and control
  • A leak site for publishing victim data
  • What appears to be a negotiation team (based on victim reports)
  • Ongoing development of their payload (version updates observed)

This level of organization typically indicates either a well-funded criminal enterprise or potential nation-state backing. Neither scenario is comforting.

Defending Against Gentlemen: A Practical Playbook

Enough doom and gloom‚Äîlet's talk about what you can actually do. Here's my practical guidance based on Gentlemen's known TTPs, organized by implementation priority:

Immediate Actions (This Week)

  • Enable HVCI: Hypervisor-Protected Code Integrity prevents most BYOVD attacks by validating driver integrity at the hypervisor level
  • Deploy Microsoft's Vulnerable Driver Blocklist: Via WDAC or Group Policy
  • Audit GPO Permissions: Who can create or modify Group Policy Objects? This list should be extremely small
  • Enable EDR Tamper Protection: Most modern EDR solutions have this‚Äîverify it's actually enabled
  • Review Domain Admin Usage: Domain Admin credentials should never be used on regular workstations

Short-Term Actions (This Month)

  • Implement Driver Allowlisting: Only permit known-good drivers to load
  • Deploy GPO Change Monitoring: Alert on any Group Policy modifications
  • Segment Privileged Access: Use PAWs (Privileged Access Workstations) for admin tasks
  • Review Backup Isolation: Can ransomware reach your backups? If yes, fix it
  • Test Restoration Procedures: Backups are worthless if you can't restore from them

Long-Term Hardening (This Quarter)

  • Implement Windows Credential Guard: Protects against credential theft
  • Deploy Application Control: WDAC or AppLocker to prevent unauthorized executables
  • Establish Threat Hunting Program: Proactively look for BYOVD and lateral movement indicators
  • Conduct Purple Team Exercises: Test your detection of BYOVD and GPO abuse

Detection Opportunities

While Gentlemen is sophisticated, they're not invisible. Here's what to monitor for:

Driver Loading Events

  • Monitor Windows Event ID 7045 (New Service Installation) for driver installations
  • Alert on drivers loaded from unusual paths (%TEMP%, user directories)
  • Maintain a baseline of expected drivers and alert on anomalies
# Splunk Query: Detect Suspicious Driver Loading index=windows EventCode=7045 ServiceType="kernel mode driver" | where NOT match(ServiceFile, "(?i)system32\\drivers") | stats count by Computer, ServiceName, ServiceFile

GPO Modification Monitoring

  • Monitor Event IDs 5136/5137 for Active Directory object modifications
  • Alert on GPO changes outside approved change windows
  • Track changes to SYSVOL (where GPO files are stored)
# Splunk Query: Detect GPO Modifications index=windows EventCode IN (5136, 5137) | where ObjectClass="groupPolicyContainer" | stats count by SubjectUserName, ObjectDN, OperationType | where NOT SubjectUserName IN ("approved_admin_list")

EDR Health Monitoring

  • Alert when endpoints stop reporting to your EDR console
  • Monitor for EDR service termination events
  • Track process termination of known security tool processes

Data Exfiltration Indicators

  • Unusual outbound data volumes, especially to cloud storage
  • Large file uploads during off-hours
  • Connections to known file-sharing services from servers that shouldn't use them

Incident Response: If You're Already Compromised

If you suspect a Gentlemen compromise (or any ransomware with similar TTPs), here's the critical first response:

Immediate Containment

  1. Isolate Affected Systems: Network isolation, not shutdown (preserve memory evidence)
  2. Disable Compromised Accounts: Especially any Domain Admin accounts that may be compromised
  3. Block Attacker C2: If you have indicators, block them at the perimeter
  4. Protect Domain Controllers: If not yet affected, isolate them immediately
  5. Preserve Evidence: Memory dumps and disk images before any remediation

Investigation Focus Areas

  • How did initial access occur? (VPN? Phishing? Exposed service?)
  • What accounts are compromised? (Check for new accounts, password resets)
  • What GPOs were modified? (Compare current state to known-good baseline)
  • What data was exfiltrated? (Check network logs, cloud service logs)
  • Are backups intact? (Verify before relying on them)

The Bigger Picture: Why Ransomware Keeps Winning

Every time I write about a new ransomware variant, I'm reminded of a fundamental truth: we're losing this battle. Not because we lack the tools or the knowledge, but because we lack the consistency in applying them.

Think about it:

  • HVCI has existed since Windows 10‚Äîmost organizations haven't enabled it
  • Driver blocklists are free‚Äîmost organizations don't deploy them
  • GPO monitoring is built into Windows‚Äîmost organizations don't alert on it
  • Immutable backups are available‚Äîmost organizations don't use them
  • We know credential theft enables lateral movement‚ÄîDomain Admins still log into workstations

The Gentlemen gang isn't using zero-days or nation-state level capabilities. They're using known techniques against known gaps. And they're succeeding because those gaps persist year after year.

The uncomfortable question every security leader should ask: "If Gentlemen targeted us tomorrow, would we detect them? Would we stop them? Or would we be calling our incident response retainer and cyber insurance carrier?"

What I'm Watching Next

A few things I'm monitoring as this threat evolves:

  • Vulnerable Driver Discovery: Security researchers continuously find new vulnerable drivers. Expect BYOVD attacks to incorporate newer drivers as they're discovered. Microsoft's blocklist will always be playing catch-up.
  • Affiliate Model Expansion: Many ransomware operations run affiliate programs, recruiting other criminals to deploy their ransomware in exchange for a cut. If Gentlemen adopts this model, attack volume could increase dramatically.
  • Linux Variants: Given their Go-based codebase, a Linux variant is trivial to produce. Organizations with mixed Windows/Linux environments should prepare for both.
  • Targeting Patterns: As more victim data emerges, we'll understand whether certain industries or regions are being specifically targeted or if this remains opportunistic.

Final Thoughts

The Gentlemen ransomware gang represents the continuing evolution of ransomware threats. They're technically capable, operationally sophisticated, and clearly well-resourced. The combination of BYOVD, GPO manipulation, and double extortion makes them a formidable adversary.

But here's what I keep coming back to‚Äîeverything they're doing is preventable. Every technique they use has corresponding defenses. The BYOVD attacks fail against HVCI-enabled systems. The GPO abuse fails if privileged access is properly controlled. The exfiltration fails if proper DLP and network segmentation are in place.

The question isn't whether we know how to defend against these attacks. The question is whether we have the organizational will to implement those defenses before we become victims.

I'll continue tracking this group and will update this article as we learn more. In the meantime, take a hard look at your driver policies, your AD security, and your data loss prevention capabilities. The Gentlemen are actively hunting, and despite their ironic name, they're not going to be polite about it.

Stay vigilant. Stay prepared. And whatever you do, don't assume it won't happen to you.
