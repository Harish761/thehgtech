Former Cybersecurity Professionals Plead Guilty to ALPHV BlackCat Ransomware Attacks

In a shocking case of insider knowledge turned malicious, an incident response manager and ransomware negotiator conspired to attack the organizations they were trusted to protect.

Attacks: May-November 2023 Guilty Plea: Dec 19, 2025 18 min read
The Ultimate Betrayal: Two trusted cybersecurity professionals—one an incident response manager, the other a ransomware negotiator—used their insider knowledge to conduct the very attacks they were hired to prevent. This case represents one of the most significant insider threat convictions in cyber history.

Executive Summary

On December 19, 2025, Ryan Clifford Goldberg and Kevin Tyler Martin pleaded guilty in the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of Florida to conspiracy to commit computer fraud for their roles in a series of ALPHV (BlackCat) ransomware attacks against U.S. organizations.

What makes this case unprecedented is the defendants' backgrounds: Goldberg was a former incident response manager at Sygnia, a leading cybersecurity firm, while Martin served as a ransomware negotiator at DigitalMint, a company that helps victims navigate ransom payments. Both used their professional expertise and insider knowledge to orchestrate attacks that caused over $9.5 million in documented losses.

The pair targeted at least five U.S. organizations between May and November 2023, including healthcare, pharmaceutical, and technology companies. One victim—a Florida medical device company—paid approximately $1.27 million in cryptocurrency ransom.

The Defendants

Ryan Clifford Goldberg
Former Incident Response Manager, Sygnia

Goldberg worked at Sygnia, a leading cybersecurity consulting firm known for incident response and threat hunting services. His role involved responding to ransomware attacks, understanding attacker tactics, and helping organizations recover from compromises—knowledge he later weaponized against victims.

Kevin Tyler Martin
Former Ransomware Negotiator, DigitalMint

Martin worked at DigitalMint, a company specializing in helping ransomware victims navigate the payment process. His role gave him intimate knowledge of victim psychology, payment mechanics, and how organizations respond to ransom demands—intelligence that proved valuable for attacking them.

ALPHV/BlackCat: The Weapon of Choice

Goldberg and Martin deployed ALPHV ransomware—also known as BlackCat—one of the most sophisticated ransomware variants in operation. Key characteristics include:

  • Written in Rust: ALPHV was the first major ransomware written in the Rust programming language, making it faster and harder to analyze
  • Triple Extortion: Encryption + data theft + DDoS threats
  • Highly Customizable: Affiliates can configure nearly every aspect of the attack
  • Proven Track Record: ALPHV has been responsible for hundreds of attacks globally

The ALPHV group operated as a Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS), meaning affiliates like Goldberg and Martin would deploy the ransomware in exchange for a percentage of any ransom payments. This model allowed them to leverage sophisticated malware without developing it themselves.

ALPHV/BlackCat Impact: Since its emergence in late 2021, ALPHV has been linked to attacks on healthcare systems, critical infrastructure, and major corporations worldwide. The FBI disrupted the group's infrastructure in December 2023, but variants continue to circulate.

Legal Consequences

Both defendants negotiated plea deals that reduced their exposure compared to the original charges:

Original Charges

The defendants initially faced multiple counts that could have resulted in up to 50 years in prison.

Plea Agreement

Under the plea deal, each defendant:

  • Pleaded guilty to one count of conspiracy to interfere with interstate commerce by extortion
  • Faces a maximum of 20 years in prison
  • Must forfeit $342,000 representing proceeds traced to their crimes
  • Awaits sentencing (dates to be scheduled)
Third Conspirator at Large: Court documents reference an unnamed co-conspirator who participated in the attacks alongside Goldberg and Martin. This individual has not been publicly identified or charged, suggesting an ongoing investigation.

How Insider Knowledge Enabled the Attacks

The defendants' professional backgrounds provided significant advantages in their criminal activities:

Goldberg's Incident Response Expertise

  • Defensive Gaps: Understanding how organizations typically detect and respond to attacks allowed him to evade common defenses
  • Recovery Prevention: Knowledge of backup strategies helped ensure victims couldn't easily recover without paying
  • Forensic Evasion: Incident response experience meant understanding how investigators trace attacks

Martin's Negotiation Knowledge

  • Victim Psychology: Understanding how victims respond to ransom demands enabled more effective extortion tactics
  • Payment Mechanics: Knowledge of cryptocurrency payment processes streamlined ransom collection
  • Pricing Strategy: Experience negotiating ransoms provided insight into how high demands could go

Implications for the Cybersecurity Industry

This case sends shockwaves through the cybersecurity community and raises critical questions about trust and vetting:

1. The Insider Threat Problem

Cybersecurity professionals have access to some of the most sensitive systems and information. When that trust is betrayed, the consequences can be devastating. This case demonstrates that even "good guys" can go bad.

2. Vetting and Monitoring

Organizations that provide cybersecurity services must implement robust vetting and monitoring of their own personnel. Key measures include:

  • Comprehensive background checks for all security personnel
  • Continuous monitoring of employee activities and access
  • Separation of duties to prevent single points of compromise
  • Behavioral analytics to detect anomalous activities

3. Client Trust Impact

Organizations hiring cybersecurity firms must now consider the risk that their defenders could become attackers. This may lead to:

  • More rigorous vendor security assessments
  • Contractual requirements for employee vetting
  • Limited access principles for third-party security providers
  • Independent audits of security service providers

Lessons Learned

Key Takeaways for Organizations:
  • Trust but Verify: Even trusted security partners require monitoring and oversight
  • Least Privilege: Limit access even for security personnel to what's strictly necessary
  • Separation of Duties: No single individual should have complete access to critical systems
  • Behavioral Monitoring: Implement user behavior analytics for privileged accounts
  • Independent Audits: Regularly audit security service providers and their personnel

Industry Reactions

"This case is a wake-up call for the entire industry. We spend so much time defending against external threats that we sometimes forget the risk from within. The very people we trust to protect us can become our greatest vulnerability." — Industry Security Executive (anonymous)

The case has prompted calls for stronger industry self-regulation, including:

  • Professional Certification Requirements: More rigorous ongoing vetting for certified security professionals
  • Industry Blacklists: Mechanisms to prevent convicted cybercriminals from working in security
  • Ethics Training: Enhanced focus on ethics in cybersecurity education and certification programs

Historical Context: Insider Threats in Cybersecurity

While this case is notable for the defendants' prominent roles, it's not the first time security professionals have crossed to the dark side:

  • NSA Contractors: Multiple cases of NSA contractors stealing classified material
  • Antivirus Developers: Instances of AV company employees creating malware
  • Penetration Testers: Cases of pentesters using access for criminal purposes
  • IT Administrators: Numerous cases of sysadmins sabotaging former employers

This case stands out because of the combination of the defendants' high-profile roles at reputable companies and the systematic nature of their attacks.
