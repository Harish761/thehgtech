Nissan Customer Data Breach: Red Hat Consulting GitLab Compromise Exposes 21,000 Japanese Customers

A breach at Red Hat Consulting's self-managed GitLab instance led to the exposure of 21,000 Nissan Fukuoka customers. The Crimson Collective threat group claimed responsibility for stealing 570GB across 28,000 repositories.

Breach: September 26, 2025 Published: Dec 23, 2025 18 min read
Supply Chain Risk Reality: This breach demonstrates how a vendor's security posture directly impacts your organization. Nissan's customer data was compromised not because of Nissan's own systems, but because of a third-party consulting engagement with Red Hat.

Executive Summary

Nissan Fukuoka Sales Co., Ltd., a Japanese subsidiary of the global automotive manufacturer, has confirmed that personal information of approximately 21,000 customers was exposed following a breach at Red Hat Consulting. The breach occurred on September 26, 2025, when threat actors compromised a self-managed GitLab Community Edition instance used by Red Hat for internal collaboration and client projects.

The attack was claimed by an extortion group known as Crimson Collective, who exfiltrated approximately 570GB of data across 28,000 internal repositories. Among the stolen data was source code, authentication tokens, and client engagement files—including the Nissan customer management system project.

Red Hat has confirmed the incident is not related to any known CVE, specifically ruling out CVE-2025-10725 (a Red Hat OpenShift AI vulnerability). The breach vector appears to be compromised credentials or an undisclosed vulnerability in the self-managed GitLab instance.

The Supply Chain Connection

This incident is a textbook example of supply chain risk manifesting in the real world. Here's how the connection worked:

  1. Nissan Fukuoka engaged Red Hat Consulting to develop a customer management system
  2. Red Hat stored project files, customer data, and source code on their internal GitLab instance
  3. Crimson Collective compromised Red Hat's GitLab, gaining access to all client projects
  4. Nissan customers became collateral damage in the broader Red Hat breach
Key Point: Nissan's own systems were not compromised. The breach occurred entirely within Red Hat's infrastructure. This is the nature of modern supply chain risk—your data is only as secure as your least secure vendor.

Data Exposed

The following types of customer data were exposed in the breach:

Nissan Customer Data (21,000 records)

  • Full customer names
  • Physical addresses
  • Phone numbers
  • Email addresses
  • Sales operations data specific to Nissan Fukuoka

Broader Red Hat Data (570GB total)

  • Source code from internal and client projects
  • Authentication tokens and API keys
  • Customer Engagement Reports (CERs) for multiple enterprises
  • Internal collaboration documents
  • Configuration files and deployment scripts
Authentication Tokens: The theft of authentication tokens is particularly concerning. These tokens could potentially be used to access other systems and services, extending the impact of the breach beyond the initial data theft.

Timeline of Events

September 26, 2025 Red Hat detects unauthorized access to their self-managed GitLab Community Edition instance. Internal investigation begins.
Early October 2025 Crimson Collective publicly claims responsibility for the breach. Threat group begins leaking samples of stolen data to pressure Red Hat.
October 2025 Red Hat identifies affected clients and begins notification process. Forensic investigation continues to determine scope and entry vector.
December 5, 2025 Nissan Fukuoka Sales Co., Ltd. officially confirms the breach impacted 21,000 of their customers. Public notification issued.
December 2025 Red Hat confirms the breach is NOT related to CVE-2025-10725 or any other known vulnerability. Investigation suggests compromised credentials as likely entry point.

Who Is Crimson Collective?

Crimson Collective is an extortion-focused threat group that has emerged as a significant player in the cybercrime landscape. Unlike traditional ransomware groups that focus on encryption, Crimson Collective specializes in:

  • Data Exfiltration: Stealing large volumes of sensitive data
  • Extortion: Threatening to leak data unless payment is made
  • Public Pressure: Releasing data samples to demonstrate capabilities
  • Enterprise Targeting: Focusing on high-value targets with valuable data

The group's focus on source code repositories and development infrastructure suggests they understand the value of intellectual property and the leverage it provides in extortion negotiations.

Technical Analysis: The GitLab Attack Vector

While Red Hat has not disclosed the exact method of compromise, their statement that the breach is unrelated to known CVEs suggests several possible attack vectors:

Possible Entry Methods

Attack Vector Likelihood Evidence
Compromised Credentials High No CVE involvement confirmed
Leaked API Token/SSH Key Medium Common in GitLab breaches
Social Engineering Medium Phishing against developers
Zero-Day Vulnerability Low Would likely be disclosed
Misconfiguration Medium Self-managed instances are complex

Why Self-Managed GitLab Is Risky

Red Hat was using GitLab Community Edition (self-managed), which comes with inherent risks compared to GitLab.com (SaaS):

  • Patching Responsibility: Organization must track and apply security updates
  • Configuration Complexity: Many security settings require manual configuration
  • Missing Enterprise Features: Community Edition lacks some security features in Enterprise
  • Authentication Management: SSO and MFA integration requires additional setup
  • Monitoring Gaps: Security monitoring must be implemented separately

Impact on Nissan

For Nissan Fukuoka, this breach creates several challenges:

Immediate Impacts

  • Customer Trust: 21,000 customers must be notified their data was exposed
  • Regulatory Compliance: Japan's APPI (Act on Protection of Personal Information) requires breach notification
  • Fraud Risk: Exposed customer data could be used for phishing or identity theft

Strategic Impacts

  • Vendor Review: All consulting engagements likely under security review
  • Data Handling Policies: Questions about what data should be shared with vendors
  • Contract Terms: Future vendor contracts may require stricter security controls
"This breach underscores a fundamental truth of modern enterprise security: you are not just responsible for your own security posture, but for the security posture of every vendor with access to your data." — TheHGTech Analysis

Lessons for Third-Party Risk Management

This incident provides clear lessons for organizations managing third-party relationships:

Best Practices for Vendor Security:
  • Data Minimization: Only share the minimum data necessary for the engagement
  • Security Assessments: Require vendors to complete security questionnaires and audits
  • Contractual Controls: Include security requirements and breach notification clauses
  • Environment Segmentation: Require vendors to isolate client data from other projects
  • Right to Audit: Reserve the right to audit vendor security controls
  • Incident Response Plans: Ensure vendors have documented breach response procedures

Questions to Ask Your Vendors

  1. Where will our data be stored, and who has access?
  2. What security certifications do you hold (SOC 2, ISO 27001)?
  3. How do you secure your development environments?
  4. What is your patch management cadence?
  5. How quickly will you notify us of a breach?
  6. Do you carry cyber insurance?

Regulatory Implications

This breach triggers several regulatory considerations:

Japan - APPI

Japan's Act on Protection of Personal Information requires:

  • Notification to the Personal Information Protection Commission
  • Notification to affected individuals
  • Documentation of the incident and remediation steps

International Considerations

If any affected customers are EU residents (unlikely but possible), GDPR requirements would apply. The broader Red Hat breach may also have implications under various national data protection laws depending on which clients were affected.

Incident Response Recommendations

For organizations concerned about similar risks:

Immediate Actions

  • Inventory all vendors with access to customer or sensitive data
  • Request security attestations from consulting partners
  • Review data sharing agreements for security requirements
  • Implement monitoring for data exposure on the dark web

Long-Term Strategy

  • Develop a formal Third-Party Risk Management (TPRM) program
  • Require vendors to use enterprise-grade, cloud-hosted solutions (vs. self-managed)
  • Implement data loss prevention (DLP) for data shared with vendors
  • Conduct periodic security assessments of critical vendors
