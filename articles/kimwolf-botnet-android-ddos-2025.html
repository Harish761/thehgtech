Kimwolf Botnet Hijacks 1.8 Million Android Devices

New hyper-scale botnet infects Android TVs, tablets, and set-top boxes. Launched 1.7 billion DDoS attack commands in just 72 hours.

Event: November 2025 Published: Dec 18, 2025 20 min read
Threat Alert: A new botnet named "Kimwolf" has infected over 1.8 million Android-based devices globally. This represents one of the largest IoT botnets discovered in 2025, with unprecedented attack velocity. Organizations should review IoT security immediately.

Executive Summary

In November 2025, security researchers discovered a massive new botnet that has quietly infected over 1.8 million Android-based devices worldwide. Named "Kimwolf" after identifiers found in its code, this botnet represents a significant evolution in IoT-based cyber threats, demonstrating unprecedented scale and attack velocity.

Unlike traditional botnets that target computers and servers, Kimwolf specifically targets consumer electronics running Android—particularly smart TVs, streaming set-top boxes, tablets, and media players. These devices are attractive targets because they're typically always-on, connected to high-speed internet, rarely updated, and their users seldom notice infections.

During a 72-hour observation period, researchers recorded the botnet issuing an astounding 1.7 billion DDoS attack commands—approximately 6,500 commands per second. This makes Kimwolf one of the most active botnets ever documented, with the potential to take down major online services and critical infrastructure.

Background: The Rise of IoT Botnets

To understand the significance of Kimwolf, it's important to place it in the context of IoT botnet evolution:

The Mirai Legacy (2016-Present)

The modern era of IoT botnets began with Mirai in 2016, which infected hundreds of thousands of IoT devices and launched devastating DDoS attacks, including one that took down major portions of the internet on the U.S. East Coast. Since then, numerous Mirai variants and new botnet families have emerged, each more sophisticated than the last.

What is Kimwolf?

Kimwolf is a newly discovered distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) botnet that specifically targets Android-based consumer electronics. The name comes from identifiers found in the malware's command and control (C2) communications, though the true identity of its operators remains unknown.

Target Device Categories

  • Android Smart TVs (45% of infections): From manufacturers including budget brands and some lower-end models from major manufacturers. These devices often run older Android versions and receive infrequent security updates.
  • Streaming Set-Top Boxes (30% of infections): Budget Android TV boxes, particularly those sold on marketplaces like Amazon, AliExpress, and similar platforms. Many of these devices ship with minimal security and some even contain pre-installed malware.
  • Android Tablets (15% of infections): Especially older devices running Android 8 or earlier that no longer receive security patches. Many are still used as "couch tablets" or kitchen devices.
  • Media Players and Streaming Sticks (10% of infections): Android-based streaming sticks and media players, including some devices designed for digital signage.

Connection to AISURU Botnet

Security researchers at multiple firms, including QiAnXin's XLab, have identified significant connections between Kimwolf and the previously documented AISURU botnet. This connection is based on several factors:

Technical Similarities

  • Shared Code Base: Approximately 40% of Kimwolf's code appears to be derived from or shares origins with AISURU
  • C2 Infrastructure: Both botnets have used overlapping command and control servers, suggesting shared infrastructure or operators
  • Communication Protocol: The encrypted protocol used for bot-to-C2 communication is nearly identical
  • Exploitation Techniques: Both target similar vulnerabilities in Android devices

What This Means

The AISURU connection indicates that Kimwolf is not the work of amateur hackers experimenting with botnet code. Instead, it represents a sophisticated operation by threat actors with established expertise in building and operating large-scale botnets. This suggests:

  • Professional operators with significant resources
  • Likely commercial motivation (DDoS-for-hire services)
  • Ongoing development and evolution of capabilities
  • Potential for future, more advanced variants

DDoS Attack Types Supported

Kimwolf supports multiple DDoS attack methodologies:

  • UDP Flood: Overwhelming targets with UDP packets to exhaust bandwidth
  • TCP SYN Flood: Exploiting TCP handshake to consume server resources
  • HTTP Flood: Legitimate-looking HTTP requests to overwhelm web servers
  • DNS Amplification: Using DNS servers to amplify attack traffic
  • Slowloris: Keeping connections open to exhaust server connection pools

Infection Mechanism: Technical Deep Dive

Kimwolf spreads through multiple infection vectors, demonstrating the sophistication of its operators:

1. Malicious APKs (Primary Vector)

The most common infection method is through trojanized Android applications distributed via:

  • Third-Party App Stores: APKPure, Aptoide, and regional stores with less security vetting
  • Pirated Content Sites: "Free" streaming apps and cracked versions of paid apps
  • Social Media: APK links shared on Telegram, WhatsApp groups, and forums
  • Malvertising: Ads that redirect to malicious download pages

2. Exposed ADB (Android Debug Bridge)

Many Android devices, especially smart TVs and set-top boxes, ship with ADB enabled and exposed to the network. Kimwolf actively scans the internet for devices with ADB on port 5555:

# Kimwolf ADB exploitation pattern
1. Scan for open port 5555
2. Connect via adb connect [IP]:5555
3. Install malware: adb install -r kimwolf.apk
4. Launch payload: adb shell am start -n [package]
5. Disable ADB or change port to prevent competition

3. Vulnerability Exploitation

Kimwolf exploits several known Android vulnerabilities in unpatched devices:

CVE-2019-16253 Samsung Smart TV RCE Various 2018-2019 models
CVE-2020-0069 MediaTek SoC vulnerability Many budget devices
CVE-2021-1961 Qualcomm privilege escalation Multiple Android devices
Various No-name device backdoors Budget TV boxes

4. Supply Chain Compromise

Perhaps most concerning is evidence that some infected devices arrived with Kimwolf or related malware pre-installed. This suggests supply chain compromise at the manufacturer or distributor level, particularly for extremely cheap devices sold primarily on international marketplaces.

Consumer Alert: If you purchased an Android TV box or streaming device for under $30-40, especially from unknown brands, there's a significant chance it shipped with malware pre-installed. Consider factory resetting and monitoring network traffic carefully.

Why IoT Devices Are Prime Targets

Smart TVs and streaming boxes are particularly attractive targets for botnet operators for several reasons:

Technical Advantages for Attackers

  • Always-On Connectivity: Unlike phones that go to sleep, smart TVs are typically always connected to high-speed home networks, providing consistent bandwidth for attacks.
  • Rarely Updated: Most consumers never update their smart TV firmware, leaving known vulnerabilities unpatched for years.
  • Invisible Infections: There's no obvious performance impact—your TV still streams Netflix while also participating in DDoS attacks.
  • Long Lifespan: TVs remain in homes for 7-10+ years, far longer than phones, providing long-term botnet membership.

Market Conditions Favoring Attackers

  • Race to the Bottom: Intense price competition leads to security shortcuts
  • No Security Standards: Unlike phones, no enforced security requirements for smart TVs
  • Fragmented Ecosystem: Thousands of manufacturers, each with different (often minimal) security practices
  • Consumer Unawareness: Most users don't consider TVs as security risks
By the Numbers:
  • 14+ billion IoT devices connected globally (2025)
  • Only 15% of smart TVs receive regular security updates
  • Average smart TV runs firmware 3+ years old
  • 80% of IoT vulnerabilities could be prevented with basic security hygiene

How to Protect Your Devices

For Consumers

  • Keep Firmware Updated: Check for and install updates for your smart TV and streaming devices regularly. Set up automatic updates if available.
  • Buy From Reputable Brands: Avoid extremely cheap, no-name brand devices. Pay a bit more for devices from manufacturers with security track records.
  • Only Install Official Apps: Use the Google Play Store or manufacturer's official app store. Avoid sideloading APKs from unknown sources.
  • Disable ADB: If you're not a developer, ensure Android Debug Bridge is disabled in developer settings.
  • Monitor Network Traffic: Use your router's traffic monitoring or apps like Fing to detect unusual network activity.
  • Factory Reset Suspicious Devices: If you suspect infection, factory reset and reconfigure without installing suspicious apps.

For Network Administrators

  • Segment IoT Devices: Place smart TVs, streaming boxes, and other IoT devices on a separate VLAN from critical systems.
  • Block ADB at the Firewall: Block inbound and outbound traffic on port 5555 (ADB).
  • Monitor for C2 Traffic: Use threat intelligence feeds to block known Kimwolf and related botnet C2 domains and IPs.
  • Implement DNS Filtering: Use DNS-based security to block malicious domains.
  • Egress Filtering: Monitor and restrict outbound traffic from IoT segments.
  • Device Inventory: Maintain an inventory of all IoT devices and their firmware versions.

For ISPs and Service Providers

  • Deploy Upstream DDoS Mitigation: Implement filtering to prevent attack traffic from reaching the internet.
  • Customer Notification: Alert customers when their devices appear to be participating in botnet activity.
  • BCP38/BCP84: Implement source address validation to prevent spoofed traffic.

Indicators of Compromise (IOCs)

Signs your device may be infected:

Device Symptoms

  • Unexplained network activity or data usage spikes
  • Device running slower than usual or occasional freezes
  • Overheating when device should be idle
  • Unusual battery drain (for portable devices)
  • Strange apps appearing in installed applications list
  • Device waking up or becoming active at unusual times

Network Indicators

  • Unusual outbound connections to unfamiliar IP addresses
  • High sustained outbound bandwidth usage
  • Connections to known malicious domains
  • Traffic on non-standard ports, especially port 5555

Future Implications

The emergence of Kimwolf signals several concerning trends for the future of IoT security:

Short-Term (2026)

  • Expect more sophisticated IoT botnets targeting consumer electronics
  • DDoS-for-hire services will become more accessible and powerful
  • Manufacturers may face increased pressure to improve IoT security

Medium-Term (2026-2028)

  • Regulatory requirements for IoT security likely to emerge (EU Cyber Resilience Act, etc.)
  • IoT security ratings and certifications may become standard
  • ISPs may take more active role in botnet mitigation
Growing Threat: IoT botnets are becoming increasingly sophisticated. With over 14 billion IoT devices connected globally, attacks like Kimwolf represent a growing threat to internet infrastructure. Organizations should prioritize IoT security in 2026 planning. The next major internet outage could be powered by the smart TV in your living room.

  4. Primary infection vectors: malicious APKs, exposed ADB, unpatched vulnerabilities
