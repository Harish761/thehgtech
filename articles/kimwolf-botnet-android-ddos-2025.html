Kimwolf Botnet Hijacks 1.8 Million Android Devices

New hyper-scale botnet infects Android TVs, tablets, and set-top boxes. Launched 1.7 billion DDoS attack commands in just 72 hours.

Event: November 2025 Published: Dec 18, 2025 6 min read
Threat Alert: A new botnet named "Kimwolf" has infected over 1.8 million Android-based devices globally. This represents one of the largest IoT botnets discovered in 2025, with unprecedented attack velocity.

What is Kimwolf?

Kimwolf is a newly discovered distributed denial-of-service (DDoS) botnet targeting Android-based consumer electronics. Unlike traditional botnets that target computers, Kimwolf specifically infects:

  • Android Smart TVs - From multiple manufacturers
  • Streaming Set-Top Boxes - Budget devices with poor security
  • Android Tablets - Especially older, unpatched devices
  • Media Players - Android-based streaming sticks

Connection to AISURU Botnet

Security researchers have identified that Kimwolf shares infrastructure and code similarities with the AISURU botnet, suggesting either a common operator or code reuse. The connection indicates this isn't an amateur operation but rather a sophisticated threat actor with existing botnet experience.

Attack Scale

What makes Kimwolf particularly dangerous is its attack velocity. During a three-day observation period in November 2025, researchers recorded:

Metric Value
Total DDoS Commands Issued 1.7 billion
Observation Period 72 hours
Commands Per Second ~6,500
Infected Devices 1.8 million+
Attack Type Volumetric DDoS

Infection Mechanism

Kimwolf spreads through multiple vectors:

  • Malicious APKs: Trojanized apps distributed through third-party app stores
  • Exposed ADB: Devices with Android Debug Bridge (ADB) exposed to the internet
  • Vulnerability Exploitation: Known Android vulnerabilities in unpatched devices
  • Supply Chain: Pre-installed malware on cheap, unbranded devices
Why IoT Devices? Smart TVs and streaming boxes are attractive targets because:
  • They're always connected to high-speed internet
  • Users rarely check for or install security updates
  • Many run outdated Android versions with known vulnerabilities
  • Infections often go unnoticed (no visible symptoms)

How to Protect Your Devices

For Consumers

  • Keep Android TV and streaming devices updated to the latest firmware
  • Only install apps from the Google Play Store
  • Disable ADB (Android Debug Bridge) when not in use
  • Avoid cheap, no-name brand streaming devices
  • Monitor your network for unusual traffic spikes

For Network Administrators

  • Segment IoT devices on a separate VLAN
  • Block outbound traffic on ADB port (5555) at the firewall
  • Monitor for unusual outbound connection patterns
  • Consider network-level IoT security solutions
  • Implement egress filtering to detect botnet C2 traffic

Indicators of Compromise

Signs your device may be infected:

  • Unexplained network activity or data usage spikes
  • Device running slower than usual
  • Overheating when device should be idle
  • Unusual battery drain (for portable devices)
  • Strange apps appearing in the background
Growing Threat: IoT botnets are becoming increasingly sophisticated. With over 14 billion IoT devices connected globally, attacks like Kimwolf represent a growing threat to internet infrastructure. Organizations should prioritize IoT security in 2026 planning.
