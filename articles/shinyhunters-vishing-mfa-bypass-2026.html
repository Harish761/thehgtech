ShinyHunters Voice Phishing: How Hackers Are Bypassing MFA in Real-Time

The phone rings. "Hi, this is IT support—we need to verify your account." Within minutes, your MFA is compromised, your SSO credentials harvested, and attackers are inside your Salesforce, Slack, and email. ShinyHunters' vishing campaign has hit 100+ enterprises and shows no signs of stopping.

Campaign: Jan 2026 Published: Jan 27, 2026 22 min read Harish G
ACTIVE THREAT: As of January 2026, ShinyHunters is conducting an ongoing vishing campaign targeting enterprises using Okta, Google Workspace, and Microsoft Entra ID for SSO. Mandiant and Okta have issued warnings about custom phishing kits capable of intercepting MFA in real-time. At least 100 organizations have been targeted, with confirmed breaches at Crunchbase, SoundCloud, and Betterment.

Executive Summary

ShinyHunters—the notorious cybercrime group behind breaches at Ticketmaster, AT&T, and Snowflake—has evolved their attack methodology. Their January 2026 campaign represents a significant escalation: voice phishing (vishing) combined with real-time MFA interception.

Unlike traditional phishing that relies on emails and links, this campaign begins with a phone call. Attackers impersonate IT support staff, guide victims to convincing fake SSO login pages, and capture credentials and MFA tokens in real-time before they expire. The result? Complete account takeover without triggering traditional security alerts.

Technical Analysis: The Attack Chain

This campaign is notable for its synchronization of social engineering with technical exploitation. Here's how it works:

1

Reconnaissance Phase

Attackers identify target organizations and employees through LinkedIn, Hunter.io, and data broker leaks. They focus on employees with access to sensitive SaaS platforms—particularly those managing SSO, Salesforce, or financial systems.

2

Initial Contact (Vishing Call)

The victim receives a phone call from someone claiming to be IT support or help desk. Caller ID is spoofed to show an internal or trusted number. The caller creates urgency: "We've detected suspicious login activity on your account—I need to verify your identity."

3

Phishing Page Delivery

The victim is directed to a fake SSO login page (Okta, Google, or Microsoft) hosted on a freshly registered domain. The phishing kit is configured to proxy requests to the legitimate SSO provider in real-time.

4

Credential Harvest

When the victim enters credentials, the phishing kit captures them and immediately submits them to the real SSO provider. The legitimate provider triggers an MFA challenge.

5

Real-Time MFA Interception

The attacker on the phone synchronizes with the phishing kit. As the MFA prompt appears (push notification, SMS, or TOTP), the attacker coaches the victim: "You should have received a verification code—please read it to me" or "Go ahead and approve that login notification."

6

Session Hijack

With credentials and MFA token captured, the attacker establishes a legitimate session. They may immediately enroll their own MFA device to maintain persistent access and invalidate the victim's MFA.

7

Lateral Movement & Data Exfiltration

Using the compromised SSO account, attackers pivot to connected SaaS applications: Salesforce, Slack, GitHub, Google Drive, etc. They exfiltrate valuable data, often including customer PII, financial records, and intellectual property.

8

Extortion

Victims receive ransom demands. If payment isn't made, data is leaked on BreachForums or Telegram, damaging reputation and triggering regulatory consequences.

The Phishing Kit: Technical Deep Dive

The custom phishing kits used in this campaign are sophisticated adversary-in-the-middle (AiTM) tools. Based on analysis from security researchers, here are the key technical characteristics:

Domain Patterns

Attackers register domains that closely mimic legitimate SSO providers:

  • okta-verify-[company].com
  • sso-[company]-login.com
  • google-workspace-verify.com
  • microsoft-entra-auth.com

Real-Time Proxy Architecture

Unlike static phishing pages that simply capture credentials, these kits function as reverse proxies:

  1. Victim's browser connects to the phishing domain
  2. Phishing server proxies requests to legitimate SSO provider
  3. Legitimate responses (including MFA challenges) are relayed back to victim
  4. Session cookies and tokens are captured server-side
  5. Attacker's browser receives captured session, bypassing MFA entirely
Why Traditional MFA Fails:
  • Push notifications: Victim approves the prompt thinking it's legitimate
  • SMS/TOTP codes: Victim reads the code to the attacker over the phone
  • Email OTP: Captured through the same session proxy
The MFA challenge is real—it's happening against the legitimate provider. The victim has no indication they're being phished.

Browser Fingerprint Matching

Advanced phishing kits capture browser fingerprints (user agent, timezone, screen resolution) and replay them when establishing the attacker's session. This helps evade anomaly detection systems that flag logins from unusual devices.

Why This Attack Succeeds: The Human Factor

Security teams often focus on technical controls: email filtering, endpoint protection, network segmentation. This attack exploits a fundamental gap: the phone channel is largely undefended.

Psychological Manipulation Tactics

Tactic How It's Used
Authority Caller claims to be IT support, help desk, or security team
Urgency "Your account is showing suspicious activity—we need to verify now"
Helpfulness Attacker is polite, professional, and "helping" resolve an issue
Consistency Login page looks legitimate, MFA prompt is real, nothing seems wrong
Social Proof "Other employees had this issue too—it's a known problem"

Why Security Training Falls Short

Most security awareness training focuses on:

  • Recognizing suspicious emails
  • Checking URLs before clicking
  • Not downloading attachments from unknown senders

Few programs adequately cover:

  • Verifying the identity of inbound phone callers
  • Understanding that MFA can be bypassed through social engineering
  • Recognizing when a "real" SSO prompt is actually proxied through an attacker
"These attacks exploit user manipulation rather than platform vulnerabilities. The human is the attack surface." — Salesforce Security Advisory, March 2025

Defense Strategies

Protecting against this attack requires a multi-layered approach that addresses both technical controls and human factors.

Immediate Actions

Deploy FIDO2/Passkeys

Hardware security keys (YubiKey, Google Titan) and device-bound passkeys are phishing-resistant by design. They cryptographically bind authentication to the legitimate domain—fake SSO pages can't trigger them.

Enable Number Matching

For push notification MFA, require users to enter a number displayed on the login screen into their authenticator app. This prevents blind "approve" attacks from voice calls.

IT Support Verification

Establish clear policies: IT will never call asking for credentials or MFA codes. Create a callback process where employees hang up and call IT through official channels.

Monitor Device Enrollments

Alert on new MFA device registrations. If an attacker enrolls their device, rapid detection allows for quick remediation before further compromise.

Long-Term Defenses

Recommended Architecture Changes:
  • Conditional Access Policies: Require managed devices and compliant security posture for SSO access
  • Impossible Travel Detection: Alert when same account logs in from geographically distant locations
  • SaaS Security Posture Management (SSPM): Monitor OAuth app authorizations and API activity
  • Privileged Access Management: Require additional verification for access to sensitive systems (Salesforce, financial apps)
  • Zero Trust Architecture: Never trust, always verify—even for authenticated SSO sessions

Training Updates

Security awareness training must evolve to include:

  • Vishing scenarios: Simulate IT support calls and test employee responses
  • MFA education: Explain that MFA is not foolproof—codes can be captured in real-time
  • Verification protocols: Teach employees to verify caller identity through separate channels
  • Reporting culture: Encourage reporting of suspicious calls without fear of embarrassment

Indicators of Compromise (IOCs)

While specific IOCs for the January 2026 campaign are still being published by threat intelligence vendors, defenders should monitor for:

Detection Indicators:
  • Login attempts from new/unknown devices immediately after phone contact
  • MFA device enrollment following password authentication
  • OAuth application authorizations with broad scopes (mail.read, files.readwrite)
  • API activity spikes in Salesforce, Slack, or Google Workspace
  • SSO logins from VPN or hosting provider IP ranges
  • Employees reporting IT support calls they didn't request

Domain Patterns to Block

Block or alert on domains matching these patterns:

*-okta-verify.*
*-sso-login.*
*-workspace-verify.*
*-entra-auth.*
login-[yourcompany]-*.*
verify-[yourcompany]-*.*

Legal and Regulatory Implications

Organizations breached through this attack vector face significant regulatory exposure:

  • GDPR: Breaches involving EU citizen data must be reported within 72 hours; potential fines up to 4% of annual global revenue
  • CCPA/CPRA: California residents have private right of action for data breaches resulting from negligent security
  • SEC Disclosure: Public companies must disclose material cybersecurity incidents within 4 business days
  • PCI-DSS: Payment card data exposure triggers compliance audits and potential fines

The use of social engineering rather than technical exploitation doesn't provide legal cover. Regulators increasingly expect organizations to defend against known attack patterns—and vishing has been documented as a threat for years.

What Comes Next

This campaign signals a broader shift in attack methodology. Expect to see:

  • AI-enhanced vishing: Voice cloning and deepfakes making calls even more convincing
  • Automation at scale: Attackers using AI to conduct simultaneous vishing campaigns against hundreds of targets
  • Targeting of FIDO2 enrollment: Attackers may focus on initial passkey registration as a new interception point
  • OAuth worm propagation: Using compromised SSO accounts to authorize malicious apps that spread across connected SaaS platforms
The Bottom Line: The combination of voice phishing with real-time MFA interception represents a significant evolution in social engineering attacks. Organizations that rely on traditional MFA without phishing-resistant factors are increasingly vulnerable. The time to deploy FIDO2/passkeys is now—before your organization becomes the next victim.
