AI-Powered Hacker Breaches 600+ FortiGate Firewalls Across 55 Countries

Harish G February 23, 2026 15 min read
Back to Articles

Executive Summary

What happened: Between January 11 and February 18, 2026, a Russian-speaking, financially motivated threat actor used commercial generative AI tools to compromise over 600 FortiGate firewall devices across 55+ countries.

How: The attacker did not exploit a FortiGate zero-day. They targeted exposed management interfaces with weak or default credentials lacking multi-factor authentication. AI was used for attack planning, script development, and post-exploitation.

Who reported it: Amazon Threat Intelligence. The findings were published on February 20, 2026 by AWS CISO CJ Moses.

Confidence Level: High — based on direct observation by Amazon Threat Intelligence.

TLP: CLEAR

This analysis is based on the AWS Security Blog (CJ Moses, Feb 20 2026), BleepingComputer, The Hacker News, Computing.co.uk, and Security Affairs reporting as of February 23, 2026. Details may evolve as the investigation continues.

1. What Actually Happened

Amazon Threat Intelligence discovered a server operated by the threat actor that contained malicious tools, AI-generated attack methodologies, stolen configurations from compromised devices, and custom tooling source code. The attacker's poor operational security (OPSEC) exposed the entire operation.

The key finding that makes this significant: the attacker was assessed as having low-to-medium technical skill. The scale of the campaign — 600+ devices across 55 countries — was achieved almost entirely through AI augmentation of an unskilled operator.

Attack Vector: Not a Zero-Day

This is critical to understand: no FortiGate vulnerability was exploited. The attacker succeeded by finding FortiGate appliances with:

  • Management interfaces exposed to the internet — a misconfiguration, not a software flaw
  • Weak or default credentials on admin accounts
  • No multi-factor authentication (MFA) on admin or VPN access
  • Single-factor authentication only for SSL-VPN connections

When the attacker encountered hardened environments (MFA enabled, management ports unexposed), they moved on to softer targets, according to Amazon's report.

2. The AI-Augmented Attack Chain

The attacker used multiple commercial generative AI services (the specific services were not named by Amazon) across every phase of the operation:

Phase 1: Reconnaissance

AI was used to generate scanning methodologies and identify exposed FortiGate management interfaces across the internet. The attacker operated opportunistically — scanning for low-hanging fruit rather than targeting specific organizations.

Phase 2: Credential Attacks

Default and weak credentials were tested against exposed management ports. No brute-force sophistication — just basic credential stuffing augmented by AI-generated scripts.

Phase 3: Configuration Extraction

Once inside, the attacker extracted full device configurations including SSL-VPN credentials, administrative usernames and passwords, firewall policies, internal network topology maps, and IPsec VPN configurations.

Phase 4: AI-Assisted Parsing

Custom Python and Go scripts — generated with AI assistance — were used to parse and decrypt stolen configurations. This is where the AI augmentation was most impactful: the attacker lacked the skill to write these tools manually.

Phase 5: Lateral Movement & Pre-Ransomware

Using extracted VPN credentials, the attacker connected to internal networks, compromised Active Directory environments, extracted credential databases, and targeted backup infrastructure — a classic pre-ransomware playbook.

3. What the AI Actually Did (and Didn't Do)

Based on Amazon's report, the AI was used for:

AI-Assisted Task What It Replaced
Generating attack methodologies Manual research and planning
Writing Python/Go parsing scripts Manual coding (attacker lacked this skill)
Decrypting FortiGate configuration files Manual reverse engineering
Planning lateral movement strategies Experienced red team knowledge
Creating reconnaissance frameworks Custom scanner development

What the AI did not do:

  • Discover or create a zero-day vulnerability
  • Operate autonomously without human direction
  • Bypass properly configured security controls (MFA, unexposed management ports)
  • Execute the final ransomware payload (unclear if ransomware was deployed)

Key Takeaway

The AI didn't make the attack more sophisticated. It made a low-skill attacker capable of operating at a scale that previously required a skilled team. The attack itself was basic — default credentials on exposed ports. The AI's contribution was automation, parsing, and planning.

4. Geographic and Sector Impact

The campaign was opportunistic, not targeted. Compromised devices were found across:

  • South Asia
  • Latin America and Caribbean
  • West Africa
  • Northern Europe
  • Southeast Asia

No specific industry vertical was targeted. The attacker scanned broadly and compromised whatever was vulnerable.

5. Detection: What to Look For

Amazon Threat Intelligence recommends auditing for the following indicators:

Detection Indicators

Check your FortiGate logs for these patterns:

  • New admin accounts created after January 11, 2026 that you didn't authorize
  • New SSL-VPN user accounts that don't match HR/IT provisioning records
  • Unusual SSH sessions to the management interface from external IPs
  • Configuration backup exports initiated outside maintenance windows
  • VPN connections from unexpected geographic locations

6. Immediate Remediation

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS (based on Amazon's recommendations):
  1. Restrict management interface access — Do not expose FortiGate management ports (443/admin, 22/SSH) to the internet. Use out-of-band management or IP allowlists.
  2. Enable MFA on all admin and VPN accounts — This single control would have stopped this entire campaign.
  3. Audit all admin and VPN accounts — Remove any accounts created after January 11, 2026 that are not authorized.
  4. Rotate all credentials — Change admin passwords and VPN pre-shared keys. Assume extracted configurations are compromised.
  5. Do not reuse VPN passwords for Active Directory — The attacker used extracted VPN credentials to pivot into AD.
  6. Harden backup infrastructure — The attacker targeted backups specifically, indicating pre-ransomware activity.
  7. Review firewall policies — Ensure no unauthorized rules were added during the compromise.

7. Why This Matters Beyond FortiGate

This campaign is the clearest documented case of AI lowering the barrier to entry for cybercrime at scale. The implications are broader than one vendor:

The Skill Gap Collapse

Historically, compromising 600+ devices across 55 countries required a well-resourced team — think APT groups with dedicated developers, infrastructure operators, and regional specialists. This threat actor accomplished it as what Amazon assessed to be an individual or small group with poor OPSEC and limited technical ability.

The AI didn't teach the attacker to hack. It wrote the tools they couldn't write themselves. It planned the lateral movement they didn't know how to plan. It parsed the configurations they didn't know how to read.

Volume is Coming

Amazon's CJ Moses explicitly warned that AI-augmented threat activity will continue to grow in volume from both skilled and unskilled adversaries throughout 2026. If a low-skill operator can hit 600 devices, high-skill operators with the same tools can hit an order of magnitude more — faster and quieter.

Basics Still Win

Here's the thing that should frustrate every security professional reading this: this entire campaign fails against properly configured environments. MFA enabled? Attacker moves on. Management port not exposed? Attacker can't connect. VPN passwords not reused for AD? Lateral movement blocked.

As Amazon states in their report, organizations should prioritize: patch management for perimeter devices, credential hygiene, network segmentation, and robust detection for post-exploitation indicators.
