Sedgwick Government Solutions Hit by TridentLocker: New Ransomware Gang Claims 3.4GB Theft

A new ransomware group called TridentLocker claims responsibility for attacking Sedgwick Government Solutions on New Year's Eve. The federal contractor confirms a cyber incident affecting its government-focused subsidiary. What we know about this emerging threat.

Attack: December 31, 2025 Published: Jan 5, 2026 18 min read By Harish G
TridentLocker New Ransomware Gang Claims Federal Contractor Attack 3.4 GB Data Allegedly Stolen from Sedgwick Government Solutions
INCIDENT CONFIRMED: Sedgwick, a global claims management company, has confirmed a cyber incident affecting Sedgwick Government Solutions, its federal contractor subsidiary. The TridentLocker ransomware group claims responsibility, alleging they stole 3.4 gigabytes of data on New Year's Eve 2025.

The Attack: New Year's Eve Strike

There's a reason cybercriminals love holidays. When security teams are reduced, response times are slower, and leadership is unavailable, attackers strike. The TridentLocker attack on Sedgwick Government Solutions exemplifies this tactical approach.

According to the ransomware group's claims and Sedgwick's confirmation:

  • Attack date: December 31, 2025 (New Year's Eve)
  • Target: Sedgwick Government Solutions (federal contractor subsidiary)
  • Data claimed: 3.4 gigabytes exfiltrated
  • Ransomware group: TridentLocker (emerging actor)
  • Parent company: Sedgwick (global claims management with $8B+ annual revenue)

What is Sedgwick Government Solutions?

Sedgwick Government Solutions is the company's federal contractor arm, providing claims management and related services to U.S. government agencies. This could include:

  • Federal employee claims: Workers' compensation for government workers
  • Disability determinations: Processing benefits applications
  • Healthcare administration: Managing government health programs
  • Disaster response: FEMA-related claims processing
Data Sensitivity: As a federal contractor handling claims, Sedgwick Government Solutions likely processes highly sensitive personal information including Social Security numbers, medical records, employment histories, and financial details of government employees and benefit recipients.

Who is TridentLocker?

TridentLocker appears to be a new entrant in the ransomware ecosystem. The group's emergence with a federal contractor attack as their debut suggests either unusual ambition or possible connections to more established operations.

What We Know

  • First appearance: Late December 2025 / Early January 2026
  • Naming: "Trident" motif suggests nautical theming (similar to other groups)
  • Initial target: High-profile federal contractor (unusual for new groups)
  • Tactics: Data exfiltration + extortion (modern double-extortion approach)

Possible Origins

New ransomware groups typically emerge through several paths:

  1. Splinter group: Members from disbanded groups like Conti, REvil, or ALPHV forming new operations
  2. RaaS affiliate graduation: Former affiliates launching their own operation
  3. Rebranding: Existing group using new name to evade law enforcement attention
  4. New actors: Genuinely new entrants (less common at this capability level)
Analysis Note: The sophistication required to successfully breach a federal contractor on their first (public) operation suggests TridentLocker may be a rebrand or spinoff rather than genuinely new actors. We're tracking for indicators linking them to known ransomware ecosystems.

The Segmentation Defense

Sedgwick's response emphasized a critical point: the Government Solutions subsidiary operates in a segmented environment, separate from the broader company infrastructure. This is exactly how defense-in-depth should work.

"Sedgwick Government Solutions is segmented, meaning no broader company systems or data were affected." — Sedgwick Official Statement

Why Segmentation Matters

  • Blast radius containment: Breach limited to one subsidiary
  • Regulatory compliance: Federal contractors often required to isolate government data
  • Recovery simplification: Isolated systems can be rebuilt without affecting operations
  • Negotiation leverage: Limited scope may reduce ransom pressure

Questions That Remain

  • What specific data types were in the 3.4GB claimed?
  • Are government employees or benefit recipients affected?
  • Has Sedgwick been in contact with affected federal agencies?

The Holiday Attack Trend

TridentLocker's New Year's Eve timing follows a well-established pattern. Major ransomware attacks increasingly target holiday periods:

Notable Holiday Attacks

  • Kaseya (July 4, 2021): REvil struck the MSP platform over Independence Day weekend
  • JBS (Memorial Day 2021): Meat processing giant hit during the holiday weekend
  • Colonial Pipeline (Mother's Day 2021): DarkSide attacked on the weekend
  • Log4j exploitation (December 2021): Attacks intensified over Christmas

Why Holidays Work for Attackers

  1. Reduced staffing: SOC teams often at skeleton crew
  2. Delayed detection: Alerts may not be investigated promptly
  3. Slow escalation: Key decision-makers unavailable
  4. Extended dwell time: Attackers can operate longer undetected
  5. Pressure to pay: Organizations want to resume operations quickly
Defensive Lesson: Organizations should maintain heightened vigilance during holidays, implement automated detection and response capabilities, and ensure incident response teams have clear escalation paths even when leadership is on vacation.

Implications for Federal Contractors

The Sedgwick attack sends a clear message to the federal contracting community:

Immediate Actions

Recommended Steps:
  • Verify network segmentation between government and commercial operations
  • Review access controls for government-related data and systems
  • Ensure incident response plans address holiday/weekend scenarios
  • Confirm backup integrity and test restoration capabilities
  • Update government contracting officers on security posture

Longer-term Considerations

  • CMMC readiness: The Cybersecurity Maturity Model Certification is increasingly required
  • Third-party risk: Assess security of subcontractors and vendors
  • Cyber insurance: Verify coverage adequacy for government contract breaches
  • Disclosure obligations: Understand reporting requirements to contracting agencies

As the investigation continues, the security community will be watching to learn more about this new player in the ransomware ecosystem.
