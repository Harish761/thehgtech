Nefilim Ransomware Operator Pleads Guilty: Ukrainian National Faces 10 Years in Federal Prison

International ransomware conspiracy unraveled as operator pleads guilty. His alleged co-conspirator—linked to three ransomware operations—remains at large with an $11 million bounty on his head.

Crimes: June 2021 onwards Guilty Plea: Dec 19, 2025 15 min read
Justice Served: Artem Aleksandrovych Stryzhak, 35, of Barcelona, Spain, has pleaded guilty to conspiracy to commit computer fraud for his role in international Nefilim ransomware attacks. He faces up to 10 years in federal prison and is scheduled for sentencing on May 6, 2026.

Executive Summary

On December 19, 2025, Ukrainian national Artem Aleksandrovych Stryzhak pleaded guilty in a U.S. federal court to one count of conspiracy to commit computer fraud. Stryzhak admitted to deploying Nefilim ransomware against computer networks in the United States and other countries, targeting high-revenue companies for extortion.

According to the U.S. Department of Justice, Stryzhak obtained access to the Nefilim ransomware code in June 2021 in exchange for 20% of any ransom proceeds. He then operated the ransomware through an online platform known as "the panel," targeting organizations with annual revenues exceeding $100 million, primarily in the United States, Canada, and Australia.

Perhaps more significantly, this case has revealed a larger conspiracy. Stryzhak's alleged co-conspirator, Volodymyr Tymoshchuk—also a Ukrainian national—remains at large and is wanted for serving as an administrator for three separate ransomware operations: LockerGoga, MegaCortex, and Nefilim. The U.S. State Department has offered an $11 million reward for information leading to his arrest.

The Defendant: Artem Stryzhak

Artem Aleksandrovych Stryzhak, 35, is a Ukrainian national who was living in Barcelona, Spain at the time of his arrest. Key details from court documents:

  • Nationality: Ukrainian
  • Age: 35 years old
  • Residence: Barcelona, Spain (at time of arrest)
  • Role: Ransomware operator (affiliate)
  • Arrested: June 2024 in Spain
  • Extradited: April 30, 2025 to the United States
  • Guilty Plea: December 19, 2025
  • Sentencing: Scheduled for May 6, 2026

Nefilim Ransomware: The Weapon

Nefilim ransomware emerged in early 2020 as a successor to the Nemty ransomware operation. It quickly became known for targeting large enterprises with multi-million dollar ransom demands.

Technical Characteristics

  • Encryption: Uses AES-128 for file encryption with RSA-2048 for key protection
  • File Extension: Encrypted files receive the ".NEFILIM" extension
  • Double Extortion: Data stolen before encryption for additional leverage
  • Initial Access: Commonly exploited RDP vulnerabilities and VPN flaws

Target Selection

According to court documents, Nefilim operators specifically targeted companies with:

  • Annual revenues exceeding $100 million
  • Operations in the United States, Canada, or Australia
  • Industries with sensitive data or operational dependencies
  • Limited cybersecurity maturity relative to their size
Revenue-Based Targeting: The $100 million revenue threshold wasn't arbitrary—it was a calculated business decision. Larger companies have more resources to pay ransoms and face greater reputational and operational damage from attacks, making them more likely to pay quickly.

Timeline of Events

The Fugitive: Volodymyr Tymoshchuk

While Stryzhak has been brought to justice, his alleged primary co-conspirator remains at large. Volodymyr Tymoshchuk, also a Ukrainian national, has been charged with serving as an administrator for not one, but three separate ransomware operations:

Ransomware Active Period Notable Characteristics
LockerGoga 2019 Targeted industrial companies, including Norsk Hydro ($75M+ damages)
MegaCortex 2019-2020 Evolved from LockerGoga, targeted corporate networks
Nefilim 2020+ High-value enterprise targeting, double extortion
WANTED $11,000,000

The U.S. State Department is offering up to $11 million through its Rewards for Justice program for information leading to the arrest or conviction of Volodymyr Tymoshchuk. If you have information, contact the FBI or your local law enforcement.

How the Operation Worked

Court documents reveal the inner workings of the Nefilim ransomware operation:

The Panel System

Stryzhak operated the ransomware through an online platform referred to as "the panel." This infrastructure provided:

  • Target Management: Tracking of compromised organizations
  • Ransom Negotiation: Communication channels with victims
  • Payment Processing: Cryptocurrency wallet management
  • Revenue Sharing: Automatic distribution of ransom proceeds

The Business Model

Nefilim operated as a Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) operation with a clear profit-sharing arrangement:

  • Stryzhak (Affiliate): 20% of ransom payments
  • Developers/Administrators: Remaining 80%

This model allowed the ransomware developers to scale their operation by recruiting affiliates like Stryzhak who would perform the actual attacks while the core team maintained the infrastructure.

International Law Enforcement Cooperation

Stryzhak's arrest and prosecution demonstrates the power of international cooperation in combating ransomware:

  • FBI Investigation: U.S. federal agents traced the attacks to Stryzhak
  • Spanish Cooperation: Spanish authorities arrested Stryzhak in June 2024
  • Extradition Success: Spain extradited Stryzhak to face U.S. charges in April 2025
  • Ongoing Pursuit: International manhunt continues for Tymoshchuk
"This prosecution demonstrates that ransomware actors cannot hide behind international borders. We will work with our international partners to bring these criminals to justice, no matter where they hide." — U.S. Department of Justice Statement

Nefilim's Impact: By the Numbers

While specific victim details weren't disclosed in Stryzhak's case, Nefilim ransomware's overall impact has been significant:

  • Global Reach: Victims across multiple continents
  • High-Value Targets: Companies with $100M+ annual revenues
  • Double Extortion: Both encryption and data theft used for leverage
  • Data Leak Site: Operated a site to publish stolen data from non-paying victims

Legal Implications

Stryzhak's Plea

By pleading guilty to one count of conspiracy to commit computer fraud, Stryzhak:

  • Faces a maximum of 10 years in federal prison
  • Avoided potential additional charges that could have increased his exposure
  • Will be sentenced on May 6, 2026
  • May face restitution orders requiring repayment to victims

Broader Legal Strategy

The prosecution of Stryzhak is part of a broader U.S. government strategy to combat ransomware:

  • Aggressive Prosecution: Seeking maximum penalties for ransomware operators
  • International Cooperation: Working with foreign governments on extraditions
  • Financial Rewards: Multi-million dollar bounties for information on fugitives
  • Asset Seizure: Targeting cryptocurrency and proceeds of crime

Lessons for Organizations

The Nefilim case offers several lessons for potential victims:

Defensive Recommendations:
  • Patch VPN and RDP: Nefilim commonly exploited vulnerable remote access infrastructure
  • Network Segmentation: Limit lateral movement capabilities for attackers
  • Backup Strategy: Maintain offline, immutable backups that can't be encrypted
  • EDR Deployment: Modern endpoint detection can catch ransomware before encryption
  • Revenue Anonymity: Consider that public revenue data makes you a target

The Message to Cybercriminals

This prosecution sends a clear message to ransomware operators worldwide:

  • No Safe Havens: International cooperation means hiding in "friendly" countries is no longer guaranteed protection
  • Long Memory: Crimes committed years ago can still result in prosecution
  • Extradition Works: The U.S. successfully extradited Stryzhak from Spain
  • Substantial Consequences: Real prison time awaits those who are caught
