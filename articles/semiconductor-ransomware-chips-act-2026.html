Silicon Under Siege: The Semiconductor Ransomware Crisis the CHIPS Act Ignores

The United States is investing $52.7 billion to bring chip manufacturing home. Ransomware groups are investing $0 to steal everything those fabs produce. Since 2022, cyberattacks targeting the semiconductor industry have surged over 600%, and the CHIPS Act has no meaningful answer.

Executive Summary

What this analysis covers: A mapping of the accelerating ransomware campaign targeting the global semiconductor supply chain, from the MKS Instruments attack in February 2023 to the Advantest breach in February 2026, and the structural cybersecurity gap within the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022.

Key finding: Of the $52.7 billion allocated under the CHIPS Act, only $500 million is designated for supply chain security — and that funding is administered by the State Department for international telecom and supply chain diversification, not domestic ransomware defense for semiconductor manufacturers.

Confidence Level: High — based on verified financial filings (MKS Instruments, Applied Materials, Microchip Technology), ransomware group leak site claims (confirmed by BleepingComputer, SecurityWeek, The Record), CHIPS Act allocation data (state.gov, NSF.gov), and the CloudSEK "Silicon Under Siege" threat report.

TLP: CLEAR

This analysis is based on publicly available sources including CSO Online, SecurityWeek, BleepingComputer, The Record, CloudSEK, the U.S. Department of State, NSF, and CISA as of February 25, 2026. Details may evolve as the Advantest investigation continues.

There is a peculiar kind of negligence unique to governments that spend lavishly on building things while budgeting nothing to protect them. The United States is in the middle of the most ambitious industrial policy experiment since the Apollo program — a $52.7 billion bet, via the CHIPS and Science Act of 2022, to repatriate semiconductor manufacturing from East Asia and secure a domestic supply of the silicon that runs every piece of critical infrastructure in the country.

Meanwhile, ransomware operators have discovered that the semiconductor industry is a spectacularly profitable target. According to a CloudSEK threat report published via CSO Online, titled "Silicon Under Siege: The Cyber War Reshaping the Global Semiconductor Industry," cyberattacks targeting the global semiconductor sector have surged over 600% since 2022. Confirmed ransomware losses in the industry have exceeded $1.05 billion since 2018.

The two facts sit next to each other uncomfortably: the largest public investment in semiconductor manufacturing in U.S. history is happening at the exact moment the industry is experiencing the sharpest escalation of cyberattacks it has ever seen. And they are barely connected.

"We are building the most advanced semiconductor fab in the Western Hemisphere with the cybersecurity posture of a regional hospital."

1. The Numbers: 600% and Counting

The CloudSEK report puts the scale of the crisis in sharp focus. The semiconductor industry is not just one of many sectors dealing with a general uptick in ransomware — it is experiencing a targeted escalation.

The drivers are straightforward:

  • Geopolitical value: Semiconductor intellectual property — chip designs, process node secrets, test methodologies — is among the most strategically valuable data on the planet. The U.S.-China semiconductor trade war has intensified the value of this data for state-aligned actors.
  • IT/OT convergence: Modern semiconductor fabrication requires tight integration between information technology (IT) systems and operational technology (OT) systems. CloudSEK's data shows that over 60% of Industrial Control System breaches in the sector originated from IT vulnerabilities — phishing, VPN exploits — before pivoting into operational environments.
  • High willingness to pay: Semiconductor fabs cannot tolerate downtime. A single day of production disruption at a major fab can cost tens of millions of dollars. Ransomware groups know this.

2. The Attack Timeline: 2023–2026

The following timeline documents every major, publicly confirmed ransomware incident targeting the semiconductor supply chain in three years. Every entry has been verified against multiple primary sources.

Date Target What Happened Confirmed Impact Source
Feb 2023 MKS Instruments Ransomware attack; operations suspended at multiple facilities. Systems encrypted, employee data potentially exfiltrated (SSNs, bank details). $200M revenue loss (Q1 2023). Downstream impact: Applied Materials reported $250M projected revenue loss from the supply chain disruption. BankInfoSecurity, CSO Online
Jun 2023 Kinmax Technology (TSMC supplier) LockBit ransomware group claimed breach. Kinmax confirmed unauthorized access to its systems. TSMC stated no business data was affected. TSMC confirmed breach was limited to supplier. CloudSEK estimated broader impact at approximately $256M across the supply chain. BleepingComputer
Jan 2024 Foxsemicon (Foxconn subsidiary) LockBit ransomware attack. Website defaced with ransom message. Attackers claimed 5 TB of data stolen, including customer and employee information. Company reported no significant operational impact. Website temporarily taken offline. SecurityWeek, The Record
Mar 2024 Nexperia Dunghill Leak (Dark Angels) ransomware group. Confirmed unauthorized access to IT servers. 1 TB of data exfiltrated. Stolen data included 371 GB of design/product data (chip schematics, trade secrets, NDAs) and 246 GB of engineering data (manufacturing technologies). BleepingComputer, SecurityWeek
Aug 2024 Microchip Technology Play ransomware group. Detected August 17. Systems isolated, production disrupted at multiple facilities. $21.4M in costs (factory underutilization charges). Employee contact info and hashed passwords confirmed exfiltrated. Play published 4 GB data archive. SecurityWeek, Cybersecurity Dive
Feb 2026 Advantest Corporation Ransomware detected February 15, 2026. Affected systems isolated. Third-party forensic investigation ongoing. No data breach confirmed as of Feb 25, 2026. No ransomware group has claimed responsibility. SecurityWeek, BleepingComputer, Infosecurity Magazine

The pattern is unmistakable. The interval between major incidents is compressing. The attackers are moving up the value chain — from suppliers (MKS, Kinmax) to direct manufacturers (Microchip, Nexperia) to the companies that test the chips themselves (Advantest).

Cascading Risk

A single attack on MKS Instruments — a supplier — caused Applied Materials to project a $250 million downstream revenue loss. The semiconductor supply chain is so tightly coupled that compromising one node can halt production across an entire ecosystem of companies and products.

3. The CHIPS Act Cybersecurity Gap

The CHIPS and Science Act of 2022 is the largest industrial subsidy program the United States has enacted in decades. Its stated goal is to reverse America's dependence on East Asian chip manufacturing and secure a resilient domestic semiconductor supply.

Here is where the money goes:

Allocation Amount Administered By Purpose
Fabrication incentives $39 billion Dept. of Commerce Grants and loans for building new chip fabs on U.S. soil
R&D and innovation $11 billion Dept. of Commerce National Semiconductor Technology Center, advanced packaging
Defense microelectronics $2 billion Dept. of Defense Military-grade chip R&D, university prototyping networks
Workforce training $200 million National Science Foundation Semiconductor-specific education and training programs
ITSI Fund (supply chain security) $500 million Dept. of State International supply chain diversification, secure telecom networks
Investment tax credits ~$24 billion IRS 25% credit for manufacturing equipment costs

The only line item that explicitly mentions "security" in the supply chain context is the International Technology Security and Innovation (ITSI) Fund — $500 million, disbursed at $100 million per year over five years through the State Department. This fund is designed for international objectives: promoting secure telecom networks in partner countries, diversifying the global supply chain, and building testing capacity in allied nations.

It is not designed to help a semiconductor company in Austin, Texas harden its Active Directory environment against a Play ransomware intrusion.

⚠️ Editorial Analysis

There is a structural assumption embedded in the CHIPS Act: that if the U.S. builds the fabs, cybersecurity will somehow follow. The Commerce Department's $70 billion in received funding requests for the $39 billion fabrication pool suggests the demand vastly exceeds supply — and cybersecurity hardening is not a condition of receiving those funds. Our analysis suggests this creates a scenario where the government is subsidizing the construction of high-value manufacturing targets while providing no proportional funding to defend them against the threat actors who are already, demonstrably, targeting the industry.

4. The Advantest Mystery

On February 15, 2026, Advantest Corporation — a Japanese semiconductor test equipment manufacturer headquartered in Tokyo with significant operations in San Jose, California — detected unusual activity within its IT network. The company confirmed it was a ransomware attack, isolated affected systems, and engaged third-party forensic investigators.

As of February 25, 2026:

  • No data breach has been confirmed. Advantest has stated it will notify affected parties if the investigation finds evidence of data exfiltration.
  • No ransomware group has claimed responsibility. This is unusual. Most financially motivated ransomware groups claim victims within 48–72 hours to begin the extortion clock.
  • The initial access vector has not been disclosed.
  • No significant operational disruption has been reported.

Advantest is not a household name. But it is a critical node in the global chip supply chain. Their automatic test equipment (ATE) is used by Intel, Samsung, and TSMC to verify semiconductors destined for 5G infrastructure, autonomous vehicles, AI accelerators, and defense systems. A sustained disruption to Advantest's operations could create manufacturing bottlenecks rippling across the entire chip ecosystem.

⚠️ Editorial Analysis: Why No Claim?

The absence of a public claim after 10 days is atypical for financially motivated ransomware operators. There are several possible explanations:

  • Active ransom negotiation is underway, and the group is maintaining silence as a condition.
  • The attackers are still performing reconnaissance and exfiltration before deploying their full payload.
  • This is a state-aligned operation where the objective is intelligence collection (IP theft), not extortion. Given the U.S.-China semiconductor export controls, Advantest's test methodology IP would be of significant value to state actors.

We cannot determine which scenario is in play. This section is speculative analysis, not confirmed intelligence.

5. Supply Chain Cascade: Why One Breach Costs Billions

The semiconductor industry operates on a model of extreme interdependence. No single company controls the full stack from raw silicon to a finished, tested chip. The supply chain looks roughly like this:

Simplified Semiconductor Supply Chain

Raw Materials → Wafer Fabrication → Chip Design (EDA Tools)
     ↓                ↓                    ↓
Equipment Makers  →  Foundries    →   Fabless Designers
(ASML, Applied     (TSMC, Samsung,   (AMD, Nvidia,
 Materials, MKS)    Intel, GlobalF)    Qualcomm)
                         ↓
                  Test Equipment  →  Assembly & Packaging  →  End Products
                  (Advantest,        (ASE, Amkor)             (Apple, Dell,
                   Teradyne)                                   Automotive)

The MKS Instruments case in 2023 demonstrated exactly how cascading failures work in this model:

  1. MKS hit by ransomware → production suspended at their Vacuum Solutions and Photonics Solutions divisions.
  2. MKS cannot ship components → Applied Materials (which depends on MKS parts for their equipment) cannot fulfill orders.
  3. Applied Materials reports $250M revenue impact from the MKS disruption in their Q2 2023 earnings.
  4. Fabs waiting for Applied Materials equipment experience production delays — costs that are never publicly disclosed but are absorbed across the ecosystem.

One ransomware attack on a component supplier. A quarter-billion-dollar downstream impact on a single customer. The total costs across all of MKS's customers were never fully quantified.

6. CIRCIA: The Regulatory Reckoning

There is one regulatory mechanism that directly addresses the cybersecurity gap in critical manufacturing: the Cyber Incident Reporting for Critical Infrastructure Act of 2022 (CIRCIA).

CIRCIA requires "covered entities" in designated critical infrastructure sectors — including critical manufacturing, which encompasses semiconductor production — to:

  • Report significant cyber incidents to CISA within 72 hours.
  • Report ransomware payments to CISA within 24 hours.

The final rule is expected to be published by May 2026. In preparation, CISA and DHS have scheduled sector-specific virtual town halls throughout March 2026:

7. Detection and Defense

Based on the TTPs observed across the semiconductor ransomware incidents documented above, the following detection and hardening guidance is applicable to any organization in the semiconductor supply chain.

7.1 Common Initial Access Vectors

Across the documented incidents, the most common initial access methods are:

  • Phishing (T1566): CloudSEK reports that phishing remains the primary initial access vector in over 60% of semiconductor sector breaches.
  • VPN and remote access exploitation (T1133): Exposed management interfaces and VPN appliances with weak or reused credentials.
  • Supply chain compromise (T1195): Attackers targeting smaller suppliers as a pivot into larger firms.

7.2 Example Sigma Detection Rule

The following rule targets lateral movement patterns consistent with the pre-ransomware reconnaissance observed in the Microchip Technology (Play) and MKS Instruments incidents.

Example Detection Rule — Suspicious Remote Service Installation

    Play, LockBit, and other ransomware groups during pre-deployment
    lateral movement — consistent with TTPs observed in semiconductor
    sector incidents (2023-2026).
    NOTE: This is an example detection rule engineered from public
    TTP analysis. It is NOT sourced from any specific vendor.
logsource:
    product: windows
    service: system

    selection:
        EventID: 7045
        ServiceName|contains:
            - 'PSEXESVC'
            - 'CSEXECSVC'

            - 'Windows Update'
            - 'Microsoft'
    condition: selection and not filter_legitimate

    - Legitimate SCCM or endpoint management deployments
    - Authorized PsExec usage by IT admins (should be whitelisted)

    - attack.execution

SIEM correlation note: For higher-fidelity detection, correlate this rule with Windows Security log EventID 4624 (LogonType 3 — network logon) occurring within 5 minutes on the same host. This correlation must be implemented at the SIEM level (e.g., Splunk transaction, Elastic EQL sequence) as Sigma does not natively support temporal cross-log correlation.

7.3 Network Segmentation

The single most effective mitigation against ransomware cascade within semiconductor environments is strict IT/OT segmentation. Given that CloudSEK's data shows 60% of OT breaches originate from IT-side vulnerabilities, the boundary between corporate IT and fab/manufacturing OT must be treated as a hard perimeter:

  • Deploy unidirectional security gateways (data diodes) at IT/OT boundaries.
  • Prohibit direct RDP, SMB, and WMI traffic from corporate networks to OT zones.
  • Maintain separate Active Directory forests for IT and OT environments.
  • Implement network-level micro-segmentation around EDA (Electronic Design Automation) workstations containing chip design IP.

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS for Semiconductor Companies

  • Audit VPN and remote access: Disable all unused VPN accounts. Enforce MFA on every remote access point.
  • Segment IT from OT: Ensure no direct path exists from corporate email endpoints to manufacturing control systems.
  • Backup verification: Test offline backup restoration procedures. Ensure backups are immutable and air-gapped.
  • Incident response readiness: Conduct a tabletop exercise specifically modeling a ransomware attack on your EDA or test infrastructure.
  • CIRCIA preparation: Register for the March 12, 2026 CISA town hall for Critical Manufacturing. Begin documenting your incident reporting procedures.
