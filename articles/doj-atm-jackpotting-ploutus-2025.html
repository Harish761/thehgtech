DOJ Charges 54 in Massive ATM Jackpotting Scheme: Venezuelan Gang Uses Ploutus Malware

In one of the largest ATM fraud prosecutions in U.S. history, 54 members of the Tren de Aragua gang face federal charges for a multi-million dollar jackpotting conspiracy using sophisticated malware.

Indictment: December 9, 2025 Published: Dec 22, 2025 17 min read
Major Law Enforcement Victory: This prosecution represents a significant blow to the intersection of organized crime and cybercrime. The indictment of 54 individuals linked to a designated foreign terrorist organization demonstrates the DOJ's commitment to pursuing complex cyber-enabled financial crimes.

Executive Summary

The U.S. Department of Justice announced this week the unsealing of a sweeping indictment charging 54 individuals in connection with a massive ATM jackpotting scheme that spanned multiple states and resulted in millions of dollars in losses. The defendants are alleged members of Tren de Aragua (TdA)—a Venezuelan criminal organization that the U.S. State Department designated as a foreign terrorist organization in July 2025.

The conspiracy involved deploying Ploutus malware to compromise ATM systems and force them to dispense cash on demand—a technique known as "jackpotting." The operation represents one of the most sophisticated and large-scale ATM fraud schemes ever prosecuted in the United States.

Who Is Tren de Aragua?

Tren de Aragua (TdA), which translates to "The Train of Aragua," originated in the Tocorón prison in Venezuela's Aragua state. What began as a prison gang has evolved into a transnational criminal organization with operations spanning South America, Central America, and increasingly, the United States.

Designation as a Foreign Terrorist Organization

In July 2025, the U.S. State Department designated TdA as a foreign terrorist organization (FTO) and announced sanctions against the group's leadership, including:

  • Hector Rusthenford Guerrero Flores (aka "Niño Guerrero") - Head of TdA
  • Five other key members of the organization's leadership

Criminal Activities

TdA's criminal portfolio is extensive and includes:

  • Drug trafficking
  • Human smuggling and trafficking
  • Extortion
  • Sexual exploitation of women and children
  • Money laundering
  • Kidnapping
  • Murder
  • And now: Sophisticated cybercrime operations
The Cybercrime Evolution: The TdA's move into ATM jackpotting represents a significant evolution for the organization. It demonstrates how traditional criminal enterprises are increasingly adopting sophisticated cyber techniques to diversify their revenue streams.

Ploutus Malware: Technical Deep Dive

Ploutus is one of the most prolific ATM malware families, first discovered in 2013 targeting ATMs in Mexico. Since then, it has evolved significantly and spread globally.

How Ploutus Works

  1. Physical Access: Attackers gain physical access to the ATM, typically by picking locks or using stolen keys
  2. Malware Installation: Ploutus is installed via USB drive or CD-ROM to the ATM's Windows-based computer
  3. Keyboard Attachment: An external keyboard is connected to the ATM's internal USB or PS/2 port
  4. Command Input: Attackers use specific key sequences to activate the malware
  5. Cash Dispensing: The malware commands the cash dispenser to release bills
  6. Collection: "Money mules" collect the cash and transfer it to the organization

Technical Characteristics

Feature Description
Target Platform Windows XP, Windows 7 (common in ATMs)
Installation Method USB, CD-ROM, or network (newer variants)
Activation Keyboard commands or SMS (some variants)
ATM Brands Targeted NCR, Diebold Nixdorf, Wincor, others
Dispenser Control Direct XFS (eXtensions for Financial Services) API manipulation
Anti-Detection Minimal footprint, memory-resident variants

Ploutus Evolution

The malware has evolved through several versions:

  • Ploutus (2013): Original version targeting Mexican ATMs
  • Ploutus.B (2014): Redesigned to target global ATM brands
  • Ploutus.D (2017): Cross-platform capabilities, improved stealth
  • Ploutus Variants (2020+): Network-enabled versions, remote activation capabilities

Timeline of the Investigation

2023-2024 ATM jackpotting incidents reported across multiple U.S. states. FBI and Secret Service begin investigation, identifying patterns suggesting organized criminal activity.
Early 2025 Investigation identifies connections to Tren de Aragua. Evidence gathering intensifies as the scope of the conspiracy becomes clear.
July 2025 U.S. State Department designates Tren de Aragua as a foreign terrorist organization. Sanctions announced against leadership.
December 9, 2025 Federal grand jury returns indictment charging 54 individuals with conspiracy to commit wire fraud, bank fraud, and other offenses.
December 20, 2025 DOJ announces the indictment publicly. Multiple arrests made; some defendants remain at large.

How the Scheme Operated

Based on the indictment and related documents, the TdA jackpotting operation followed a sophisticated, multi-tier structure:

Tier 1: Technical Operators

Skilled individuals responsible for:

  • Obtaining and customizing Ploutus malware
  • Identifying vulnerable ATM models
  • Creating installation media
  • Training operatives on malware deployment

Tier 2: Field Teams

Groups deployed to physically compromise ATMs:

  • Surveillance of target ATMs
  • Physical access (lock picking, key procurement)
  • Malware installation
  • Cash extraction coordination

Tier 3: Money Mules

Individuals responsible for:

  • Collecting dispensed cash at compromised ATMs
  • Transporting cash to consolidation points
  • Converting cash to cryptocurrency or other formats

Tier 4: Money Laundering

Financial operatives handling:

  • Cryptocurrency conversion
  • Wire transfers
  • Transfer of funds to TdA leadership

Protecting ATM Infrastructure

The Ploutus attacks highlight ongoing vulnerabilities in ATM security. Financial institutions and ATM operators should implement:

Physical Security

  • Access Controls: Improve physical security of ATM cabinets
  • Tamper Detection: Sensors to detect cabinet opening
  • Camera Systems: Monitor ATM areas 24/7
  • Key Management: Strict control of ATM access keys

System Hardening

  • USB/CD Lockdown: Disable unused ports and drives
  • Application Whitelisting: Only allow authorized software to run
  • Full Disk Encryption: Protect against offline attacks
  • Secure Boot: Prevent unauthorized boot media

Network Security

  • Network Segmentation: Isolate ATM networks from corporate infrastructure
  • Encrypted Communications: Secure all ATM-to-host traffic
  • Anomaly Detection: Monitor for unusual dispense patterns

The Bigger Picture: Transnational Cybercrime

This case illustrates several concerning trends in the criminal landscape:

1. Cybercrime-Organized Crime Convergence

Traditional organized crime groups are increasingly adopting sophisticated cyber techniques. TdA's move into ATM malware shows how criminal organizations can quickly acquire technical capabilities to expand their operations.

2. Transnational Operations

Modern criminal networks operate across borders, making investigation and prosecution complex. This case required coordination between multiple federal agencies and potentially international partners.

3. Critical Infrastructure Targeting

ATM networks are part of financial critical infrastructure. Attacks on these systems undermine public trust in financial institutions and can have ripple effects throughout the economy.
