Ledger Customers Exposed in Global-e Supply Chain Attack: What You Need to Know

Your Ledger hardware wallet is secure. Your crypto is safe. But your personal data—name, email, address, order history—is now in the hands of attackers. Here's what happened and what to do next.

Disclosed: January 2026 Published: Jan 12, 2026 18 min read By Harish G
Supply Chain Breach Global-e (payment processor) compromised — Ledger customer data exposed
CRITICAL ALERT: If you've purchased a Ledger hardware wallet, your personal information may be compromised. While your crypto remains secure, attackers now have the data they need to launch highly targeted phishing attacks. Be extremely suspicious of any communication claiming to be from Ledger.

Executive Summary

In January 2026, Ledger—the leading hardware cryptocurrency wallet manufacturer—confirmed that customer data was exposed due to a security incident at their third-party e-commerce payment processor, Global-e. This is a classic supply chain attack: the target (Ledger) wasn't directly breached, but their trusted vendor was.

The exposed data includes personally identifiable information (PII) such as names, email addresses, phone numbers, postal addresses, and order details. Critically, no financial data, cryptocurrency holdings, passwords, or 24-word recovery phrases were compromised.

What Exactly Happened?

Global-e is a cross-border e-commerce platform that handles payment processing for hundreds of international brands—including Ledger. When you purchase a Ledger device from the official store, Global-e processes the transaction and stores customer data necessary for order fulfillment.

The Attack Chain

1

Attacker Targets Global-e

Instead of attacking Ledger directly (which has strong security), attackers targeted their payment processor.

2

Unauthorized Access Gained

Attackers gained access to Global-e's systems containing customer order data from multiple brands.

3

Customer Data Exfiltrated

PII including names, email addresses, phone numbers, postal addresses, and order details were stolen.

4

Ledger Customers Affected

Anyone who purchased a Ledger device through the official store potentially had their data exposed.

Why Your Crypto is Still Safe: Ledger hardware wallets store your private keys in a secure element chip on the device itself. This data never touches Ledger's servers, Global-e's systems, or anything connected to the internet. The breach exposed shopping data, not cryptographic secrets.

The Real Threat: Phishing Attacks

While your crypto is safe, you are now a high-value phishing target. Attackers know:

  • You own cryptocurrency (you bought a hardware wallet)
  • You value security (you bought a Ledger specifically)
  • Your email address (direct contact vector)
  • Your physical address (for realistic social engineering)
  • What you purchased (device model, timing)

This creates the perfect storm for highly targeted spear-phishing attacks.

Expected Phishing Tactics

Example Phishing Email:

Subject: Urgent: Security Update Required for Your Ledger Nano X

Dear [Your Real Name],

Due to the recent Global-e security incident, we need you to verify your device at [malicious-link]. Your Ledger Nano X (ordered on [actual date]) may be affected.

This is a FAKE email - Ledger will NEVER ask you to verify your device via email.

Example Vishing Call:

"Hi [Your Name], this is the Ledger security team calling about the breach. We see you ordered a device to [your real address]. We need to walk you through updating your firmware to protect your assets..."

This is a FAKE call - Ledger will NEVER call you about security updates.

Example Physical Mail Attack:

[Package arrives at your real address with "Ledger" branding]
Contains a modified Ledger device with malware pre-installed, along with instructions to "replace your compromised device."

This is FAKE - Ledger will NEVER send unsolicited replacement devices.

How to Protect Yourself

Immediate Actions:
  1. Your recovery phrase is your ONLY concern. If anyone asks for it, it's a scam. Period.
  2. Ignore all "security update" emails. Genuine firmware updates come through Ledger Live, not email links.
  3. Verify sender addresses carefully. @ledger.com vs @ledger-security.com (fake)
  4. Don't answer calls about your Ledger. Ledger doesn't call customers.
  5. Be suspicious of unexpected packages. Don't use devices you didn't order.

What Ledger Will Never Do

Ledger Will Never: Why It's a Red Flag
Ask for your 24-word recovery phrase This would allow complete theft of your funds
Send you a replacement device unsolicited Modified devices can steal credentials
Call you about security issues All support is through official channels
Ask you to download software from email links Malware distribution vector
Request remote access to your computer Complete system compromise

Ledger's History with Breaches

Unfortunately, this isn't Ledger's first data incident. Understanding the history provides context:

July 2020 Marketing database breach exposed 1 million+ email addresses and 272,000 customer details including names/addresses
December 2020 Full database leaked on hacking forums, triggering massive phishing campaigns
December 2024 ESA (European Space Agency) web store breach affects Ledger customers (separate incident)
January 2026 Global-e payment processor breach exposes current customer data

The pattern is clear: Ledger's core technology remains secure, but their e-commerce and marketing operations have been repeatedly targeted. This is the inherent risk of purchasing security products online—the transaction creates a data trail.

The Bigger Picture: Supply Chain Risk

This breach illustrates a fundamental challenge in modern cybersecurity: you're only as secure as your least secure vendor.

Global-e's Other Clients

Global-e processes payments for hundreds of brands. While primarily focused on Ledger, the breach potentially affects customers of other companies using Global-e, including reports of:

  • Netflix merchandise stores
  • Disney online shops
  • Adidas international orders
  • Various luxury brands

The full scope of affected companies hasn't been publicly disclosed. If you've purchased from any international e-commerce store recently, exercise heightened caution.

Supply Chain Security Lessons

For Security Teams:
  • Vendor risk assessment must include payment processors and fulfillment partners
  • Data minimization — only share what vendors absolutely need
  • Contractual requirements for breach notification timelines
  • Third-party security audits for any vendor handling customer PII

Ledger and Global-e's Response

Both companies have issued public statements:

"Our investigation confirmed that Ledger's own infrastructure, hardware, software, and platform were not compromised. Customer cryptocurrency assets remain fully secure." — Ledger Official Statement

Global-e is directly notifying affected customers and has engaged forensic investigators. Both companies warn customers to be vigilant against phishing attempts.

Key Takeaways

Summary:
  1. Your crypto is safe — hardware wallet security was not compromised
  2. Your personal data is exposed — name, email, address, order history
  3. Phishing is the primary threat — expect targeted attacks
  4. Never share your recovery phrase — with anyone, ever, for any reason
  5. Verify through official channels — Ledger Live app, ledger.com directly
  6. Supply chain attacks are increasingly common — your vendors are attack surfaces
